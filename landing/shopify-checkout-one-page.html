Shopify One-Page Checkout: How to Enable It, What the Conversion Data Shows, and What Can Break

Shopify made one-page checkout the default for all new stores in December 2023. Older stores still run three-step checkout by default. This guide covers the switch, the real conversion impact, and the failure modes unique to the one-page layout.

Updated June 2026 All Shopify plans DTC and B2B covered
Background

What Shopify One-Page Checkout Is (and Why It Exists)

Shopify's traditional checkout was a three-step flow: a customer would move through three separate pages — contact information and address, shipping method selection, and payment. Each page was a distinct URL, a distinct page load, and a distinct opportunity for a buyer to drop off, hit a slow load, or encounter a technical error.

In August 2023, Shopify launched one-page checkout as an option across all plans. In December 2023, it became the default layout for all new stores. The change consolidates all three steps into a single scrollable page — a buyer sees the contact, shipping, and payment fields together, filling them in top to bottom without a page transition between steps.

Timeline

August 2023: One-page checkout launched as opt-in for all stores on all plans.

December 2023: One-page checkout becomes the default for all new Shopify stores.

August 2024: checkout.liquid deprecated for non-Plus stores — legacy checkout customizations stop working. All non-Plus stores use Checkout Extensibility only.

2026 and beyond: Shopify's checkout roadmap (new payment methods, extensibility features, checkout UI improvements) is one-page-first. New capabilities reach one-page checkout first or exclusively.

For older stores created before December 2023, the default layout is still three-step unless explicitly changed. Many merchants don't know the one-page option exists, or have heard of it but haven't switched because they're uncertain about the conversion impact or the risk of breaking their setup.

Visual Difference

On desktop, one-page checkout shows the full form on the left with a sticky order summary sidebar on the right. The three-step checkout shows only the current step's fields and a progress indicator at the top. On mobile, one-page checkout shows the order summary as a collapsible accordion at the top, with the full form below — buyers scroll through address, shipping, and payment in one continuous experience rather than tapping "Continue" between pages.

Setup Guide

How to Enable One-Page Checkout on Shopify

The setting is in Shopify's checkout settings. Note: if you don't see the layout option, your store may already be on one-page checkout by default (stores created after December 2023).

01
Go to Shopify Admin → Settings → Checkout
02
Scroll to the "Checkout layout" section
03
Select "One-page checkout" (the other option is "Three-page checkout")
04
Click Save. The change is immediate — test it in an incognito/private window right away
05
Test your most common purchase scenarios: standard product, product with discount code, subscription product (if applicable), Apple Pay / accelerated checkout
Plan Note

One-page checkout is available on every Shopify plan — Basic, Shopify, Advanced, and Plus. Switching between layouts is free and immediate. There is no plan restriction.

How to Switch Back to Three-Step Checkout

The same setting works in reverse. If you switch to one-page and encounter issues, go back to Admin → Settings → Checkout → Checkout layout → select "Three-page checkout" → Save. This is a safe rollback — switching back to three-step restores the previous behavior immediately and is the recommended diagnostic step if you're troubleshooting one-page issues.

Conversion Data

What the Conversion Data Actually Shows

Shopify's announcement of one-page checkout cited a 1.6x conversion rate improvement based on internal A/B test data. This figure is often quoted in third-party guides but needs context: it was measured across a broad range of stores during the initial rollout, and not all store types see the same effect.

Where the Lift Comes From

The conversion improvement from one-page checkout comes from two categories:

  • Fewer page loads = fewer load failures. Each step transition in three-step checkout is a page load with a potential timeout, JavaScript error, or slow response from a payment gateway. One-page checkout has one page load for the entire flow. Eliminating two mid-flow page loads meaningfully reduces the rate of "checkout not loading" type failures, particularly on mobile with variable connection quality.
  • Reduced cognitive load on mobile. On mobile, three-step checkout requires three tap-to-continue interactions and three page scrolls-to-top after each transition. One-page eliminates these interruptions. Shopify's research (and independent CRO research by firms like Baymard Institute) shows that mobile checkout abandonment is strongly correlated with the number of steps and transitions in the flow.

Who Benefits Most

  • High mobile traffic stores: If 60%+ of your checkout traffic is mobile, the single-page load and continuous scroll model is a meaningful improvement.
  • High-velocity impulse purchases: Low-AOV, high-volume stores where buyers want to complete checkout quickly benefit from fewer barriers between "enter card" and "order placed."
  • Cold traffic from ads: First-time buyers from paid ads have higher drop-off at each step transition. Fewer transitions = fewer drop-off opportunities.

Who Sees Little or No Lift

  • B2B and wholesale stores: Buyers are often on desktop, comparing shipping options carefully before committing to payment. The deliberate step-by-step flow can work better for this purchase behavior.
  • High-AOV stores: Luxury goods, furniture, or high-ticket categories where buyers expect a deliberate, multi-step process. Rapid single-page checkout can feel incongruent with a considered purchase.
  • Subscription-heavy stores: Subscription checkout may have extra enrollment steps that work better in a staged flow.
Important Caveat

Shopify's cited 1.6x improvement is a relative uplift across a large, heterogeneous sample — it does not mean your store will see a 60% increase in checkout conversion. Real-world results in independent merchant tests are typically 0.3–1.5% absolute improvement in checkout conversion rate. For a store converting at 3%, that means moving to 3.03–4.5%. Still meaningful at scale, but not a silver bullet.

If you want to measure the actual impact for your store, the only reliable method is an A/B test with statistical significance — which requires Shopify Plus (the only plan that offers native checkout A/B testing). On standard plans, switching and comparing before/after periods is confounded by seasonal and traffic mix changes.

Failure Modes

Why One-Page Checkout Breaks: 5 Causes Unique to the Layout

One-page checkout introduces failure modes that don't exist on three-step checkout. If you switched to one-page and saw an increase in checkout errors or a drop in conversion, one of these is the likely cause.

Cause 01
Apps Built for checkout.liquid (Three-Step Only)
Apps that customized checkout using checkout.liquid — the old template system deprecated in August 2024 for non-Plus stores — were built for the three-step URL structure and page-load cycle. If such an app is still installed (even if the code is no longer actively running), its scripts can conflict with one-page checkout rendering. Symptoms: payment section fails to appear, checkout buttons don't respond, or the page loads partially and stops. Fix: remove all apps that use checkout.liquid customizations and replace with Checkout UI Extensions where needed.
Cause 02
Pixel and Tracking Scripts That Fire on URL Change
Analytics pixels (Facebook Pixel, TikTok Pixel, Google Tag Manager custom tags) often track checkout funnel progress by listening for URL changes: the transition from /checkout to /checkout/shipping to /checkout/payment. On one-page checkout, these URL transitions don't happen — the URL stays at /checkout/ — so funnel tracking events for "reached payment step" or "initiated checkout" may fire incorrectly or not at all. This doesn't break the purchase, but it breaks your conversion reporting and can cause your ad platforms to optimize incorrectly. Fix: switch to event-based tracking using Shopify's checkout events API or Shopify's native server-side pixel (available via Web Pixels in the Shopify admin — the recommended approach for one-page checkout).
Cause 03
Checkout UI Extensions Not Placed Correctly for One-Page Layout
Checkout UI Extensions can be placed at specific "slots" in the checkout layout. The slot names and positions differ between one-page and three-step layouts. An extension placed at "checkout::dynamic::render" may appear in the correct location on three-step but in an incorrect or missing location on one-page, because the sectioning is different. Symptoms: custom fields, upsell blocks, or trust badge blocks disappear or appear in unexpected positions after switching. Fix: verify extension placement in Admin → Settings → Checkout → Customize, and reassign extension positions to the correct one-page slot names per Shopify's developer documentation.
Cause 04
Theme Scripts That Intercept Checkout Navigation
Some themes — particularly older premium themes and heavily customized themes — include JavaScript that intercepts the checkout navigation event (the click on "Continue to shipping" or "Complete order") to add custom validation or UI behavior. These scripts assume the checkout is multi-page and may block or conflict with one-page checkout's in-page form submission. Symptoms: clicking the payment button causes no visible action, or the page flickers without completing the order. Diagnosis: switch to a default Shopify theme (Dawn, Craft, or similar) and test one-page checkout — if it works, the issue is in your theme's JavaScript. Fix: identify and remove or update the conflicting theme script.
Cause 05
Discount Code Field Behavior Differences
One-page checkout and three-step checkout handle the discount code field differently. On one-page, the discount code field is visible and interactive from the start of the checkout flow — customers can apply a code before filling in their shipping address. Some discount logic (particularly discount codes that are conditional on product count, cart total after taxes, or customer segment) can behave unexpectedly when applied earlier in the flow than the three-step sequence expected. Symptoms: discount codes that worked on three-step checkout show as invalid or show incorrect amounts on one-page. Fix: test all active discount code types after switching — percentage discounts, fixed amount discounts, free shipping codes, BOGO codes, and customer-segment-specific discounts.
Monitoring

What to Monitor Differently After Switching to One-Page Checkout

Switching checkout layouts changes what you need to watch for in ongoing operations. Three areas require updated monitoring:

1. Checkout Error Rate (Not Just Abandonment Rate)

One-page checkout errors are harder to catch from analytics alone because there are no mid-flow URL changes to track. A buyer who reaches the payment step and encounters a silent error (payment fields not rendering, 3DS authentication loop, Apple Pay not initializing) looks identical to a buyer who voluntarily abandoned. The only way to catch these failures reliably is external checkout monitoring — running a synthetic transaction from outside your store on a regular interval to confirm the checkout completes successfully end-to-end.

2. App Compatibility After Updates

Apps that are compatible with one-page checkout today may break after a Shopify platform update or after the app itself updates. Unlike three-step checkout where failures are often localized to one step, a one-page conflict can affect the entire checkout flow. Monitor checkout health continuously — don't assume it's working because it worked at launch.

3. Accelerated Checkout Button Failures

Apple Pay, Google Pay, and Shop Pay buttons appear at the top of one-page checkout (above the form) as well as in the sticky summary section on desktop. These accelerated checkout paths use a different technical flow than the standard form and can fail independently of the standard payment fields. A monitoring tool that only tests the standard card form won't catch Apple Pay failures — and Apple Pay is the most common mobile payment method for high-intent buyers.

Decision Guide

Should Your Store Switch to One-Page Checkout?

For most DTC stores, the answer is yes — and the longer you wait, the more you diverge from Shopify's investment direction. But the decision depends on your stack, your buyers, and your risk tolerance for a layout change.

Switch to One-Page if:

  • The majority of your checkout traffic is mobile
  • You sell to consumers (DTC) with no B2B segment
  • Your store doesn't rely on step-by-step checkout funnel analytics for optimization decisions
  • Your app stack is modern (Checkout UI Extensions, not checkout.liquid)
  • You don't have complex conditional shipping rate logic
  • You want access to future Shopify checkout features

Consider Staying on Three-Step if:

  • You have a significant B2B or wholesale segment that carefully reviews shipping before paying
  • You have custom checkout.liquid apps that haven't been migrated to Checkout Extensibility
  • Your step-by-step funnel analytics are essential to your CRO process and you don't have an alternative measurement solution
  • You sell high-AOV products where a deliberate multi-step process matches buyer expectations
  • You have a complex subscription checkout flow where staged enrollment is important
Migration Approach

If you want to switch but are uncertain, the safest approach is:

1. Run a full checkout test on your current (three-step) setup to establish a baseline — what works, what apps and discounts are active.

2. Switch to one-page in a low-traffic window.

3. Immediately test the same scenarios: standard purchase, discount codes, subscription (if applicable), Apple Pay.

4. Monitor checkout error rates for 48–72 hours before declaring the switch stable.

5. If you see issues, switch back to three-step and diagnose — don't leave customers on a broken checkout while you investigate.

FAQ

Common Questions About Shopify One-Page Checkout

How do I enable Shopify one-page checkout?

Go to Admin → Settings → Checkout → scroll to "Checkout layout" → select "One-page checkout" → Save. Available on all plans. If you don't see the layout toggle, your store may already be on one-page checkout by default (new stores created after December 2023 start on one-page). Test in an incognito window after saving.

What is the difference between one-page and three-step Shopify checkout?

Three-step checkout spreads contact, shipping, and payment across three separate pages with distinct URLs. One-page checkout puts all three steps on a single scrollable page with one URL (/checkout/). One-page has fewer page loads (better mobile experience) but less granular step-by-step abandonment analytics in Shopify's native reporting.

Does Shopify one-page checkout increase conversion?

Shopify's internal data reported a 1.6x improvement in their rollout tests. In real-world independent merchant testing, typical results are 0.3–1.5% absolute improvement in checkout conversion rate. Mobile-heavy DTC stores see the most benefit. B2B, high-AOV, and subscription stores see smaller or no improvement. The only way to measure the actual impact for your store is an A/B test (requires Plus) or careful before/after analysis with controlled traffic.

Why did my checkout break after switching to one-page?

The most common causes: apps built for the legacy checkout.liquid system that conflict with the one-page layout; analytics/tracking pixels that fire on URL changes (which no longer happen on one-page); Checkout UI Extensions placed at slot positions designed for three-step; theme JavaScript that intercepts checkout navigation events; or discount code edge cases from earlier field availability. The fastest diagnostic: switch back to three-step checkout temporarily to confirm the issue is one-page specific, then investigate by disabling apps one at a time.

Can I A/B test one-page vs. three-step checkout on Shopify?

Native Shopify A/B testing for checkout layout requires Shopify Plus. On standard plans (Basic, Shopify, Advanced), the checkout layout is applied store-wide — you can't serve some customers one-page and others three-step. If you're on a standard plan and want to measure the impact, you'll need to switch fully and compare a before/after period — with the caveat that seasonal, campaign, and traffic mix differences will confound the comparison. For a precise measurement, Plus is the right tool. If you're not on Plus and don't want to switch without data, the pragmatic approach is to switch and monitor checkout error rates and overall conversion rate for 2–4 weeks, with a clear rollback plan if either metric declines.
