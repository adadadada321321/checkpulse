Shopify Openpay Not Working — Not Showing, Not Available, Plan Failed

Updated June 2026 · 7 failure modes · AU and NZ merchants & customers

Openpay is one of the most distinctive BNPL products on Shopify — unlike fixed-schedule competitors, Openpay lets merchants configure their own plan parameters: minimum and maximum purchase amounts, number of installments, and repayment frequency. That flexibility creates failure modes no other BNPL has. A cart value outside your merchant-configured limits hides Openpay entirely. An unexpected per-plan establishment fee triggers checkout abandonment. A customer who switched banks months ago finds their direct debit failing on instalment three. This guide covers all 7 Openpay failure modes with exact diagnostic steps.

Why Openpay Fails Differently From Other BNPL

Openpay plans are merchant-configured, not fixed. Unlike Afterpay's fixed four fortnightly payments or Laybuy's six weekly payments, Openpay merchants negotiate their own plan parameters during onboarding: minimum purchase amount, maximum purchase amount, number of installments, and repayment frequency. This means two Shopify merchants with Openpay enabled can have completely different plan limits — what works at one store may silently fail at another. A cart outside your specific merchant-configured range will not display Openpay at checkout, with no error message explaining why to the customer.

Openpay charges a per-purchase establishment fee. This is fundamentally different from "buy now, pay later" products marketed as zero-fee. Openpay customers pay an establishment fee when starting a new plan — the fee varies by plan type and duration. Customers who arrive at checkout expecting a fee-free split-payment experience and encounter an establishment fee line item on the plan selection screen frequently abandon the transaction. Merchants who don't communicate this upfront see higher BNPL abandonment rates than with other BNPL integrations.

Openpay targets higher-value and service-based purchases. Unlike Afterpay or Laybuy (primarily everyday retail), Openpay is common in dental practices, optical stores, automotive workshops, and home improvement — industries where orders are AU$500 to AU$20,000. The direct debit repayment collection model combined with longer repayment periods creates a different failure pattern: a missed payment at week 10 of a 24-week plan is far more consequential than a missed fortnight-two payment for Afterpay.

1

Openpay Not Showing at Shopify Checkout

Merchant-side

Openpay is a third-party alternative payment provider — it is not built into Shopify Payments and can silently disappear from checkout for configuration, currency, or plan limit reasons that standard payment methods never encounter.

Currency and country are the most common cause. Openpay primarily operates in Australia and New Zealand. It only appears at checkout when the store currency is AUD (Australia) or NZD (New Zealand) and the customer's shipping address is in a supported country. A store selling in USD, or a multi-currency store presenting AUD as a currency conversion rather than the fixed checkout currency, will not display Openpay regardless of configuration.

Merchant plan limits are a silent suppressor. Unlike Afterpay, which simply does not appear when a cart is over its threshold, Openpay's merchant-configured limits can hide the payment option when the cart is below the minimum — with no visible explanation to the customer. If your Openpay plan has a AU$200 minimum, a AU$180 cart shows no Openpay option and gives the customer no indication why. This is frequently misread as an outage.

Other causes: the Openpay app is installed from the Shopify App Store but not enabled in Shopify Admin → Settings → Payments → Alternative payment providers; the merchant's Openpay account is pending approval or has been suspended; or test/sandbox API credentials are still configured instead of live production keys.

How to Diagnose

  1. Check store currency per market: Shopify Admin → Settings → Markets → Australia → confirm AUD is the fixed checkout currency — not a runtime conversion from USD or another base currency
  2. Check Shopify payment settings: Shopify Admin → Settings → Payments → Alternative payment providers — confirm Openpay is listed and active, not just installed
  3. Check merchant account status: Log into the Openpay merchant portal and confirm your account is approved and live — accounts pending review cannot process transactions
  4. Check API credentials mode: In the Openpay app settings, confirm Live API keys are configured — test/sandbox credentials suppress Openpay at live checkout
  5. Test cart value against plan limits: Log into the Openpay merchant portal and check your configured minimum and maximum purchase amounts — test with cart values both above the minimum and below the maximum to confirm the plan range is correct
  6. Test with regional address: In checkout, enter an Australian or New Zealand shipping address to verify Openpay appears for that market's currency
  7. Test in incognito: Open a private browser window and complete checkout — rules out browser extension or session cache conflicts
Plan limit tip: Review your Openpay merchant plan configuration annually. Merchants whose product range has expanded upward (e.g., added high-value items after initial setup) often find their Openpay maximum purchase limit is lower than their current cart values — causing Openpay to silently hide for their best-margin orders.
2

"Not Available for This Order" — Cart Outside Merchant Plan Limits

Merchant-side

This is Openpay's most distinctive failure mode — one that does not exist with fixed-schedule BNPL products like Afterpay or Laybuy. When Openpay displays a "not available for this order" message, or when Openpay disappears from the checkout payment options without explanation, the most likely cause is that the cart value is outside the purchase amount range configured in the merchant's Openpay plan.

Every Openpay merchant has a configured plan range. During Openpay merchant onboarding, the merchant and Openpay agree on a minimum and maximum purchase amount for the payment plan. A AU$75 minimum and AU$5,000 maximum is a common configuration for mid-market retail. A dental practice might have AU$200 minimum and AU$20,000 maximum. These limits are fixed in the Openpay merchant agreement and do not automatically adjust as your store's product range evolves.

The failure pattern: A Shopify store launched with an average cart value of AU$300, configured an Openpay plan accordingly, and grew into higher-value product categories over time. Carts above AU$5,000 now receive no Openpay option — not because Openpay doesn't support the amount, but because the merchant's plan was never updated to accommodate the new range. Similarly, a store that added low-cost accessories may find Openpay invisible for those small carts because they are below the configured minimum.

To change your Openpay plan limits, you must contact Openpay's merchant support team — the limits are part of your merchant agreement and cannot be self-served in the merchant portal. Openpay may require a plan review for significant limit increases.

Fix Steps for Merchants

  1. Check your current plan limits: Log into the Openpay merchant portal — your configured minimum and maximum purchase amounts should be visible in your account or plan settings
  2. Audit your Shopify order values: Shopify Admin → Analytics → Reports → Average order value — compare your actual cart distribution against your Openpay plan limits to identify gaps
  3. Contact Openpay merchant support: If your plan limits no longer match your product range, contact Openpay to request a plan review and limit adjustment — include your current AOV and maximum cart values
  4. Add checkout messaging for out-of-range carts: Add cart or checkout notes explaining Openpay is available for purchases between your configured minimum and maximum — reduces customer confusion when Openpay is invisible for certain cart sizes
  5. Test with carts at the boundary: After any plan change, test checkout with cart values at exactly the minimum, at the maximum, one dollar below the minimum, and one dollar above the maximum to confirm the limits are active as expected
  6. Consider plan structure for high-AOV stores: For stores selling above AU$20,000, confirm with Openpay whether a higher-limit plan or a Latitude-backed finance product is available for your merchant category
Silent failure warning: When a cart is below your configured Openpay minimum, Openpay does not show an error — it simply does not appear in the payment options list. Customers with sub-minimum carts have no way of knowing Openpay is available for larger purchases. This is different from an error message and is frequently reported as "Openpay has stopped working."
3

New Customer Identity Verification Failure

Customer-side

Openpay requires all new customers to complete identity verification before their first purchase. This Know Your Customer (KYC) process checks the customer's name, date of birth, residential address, and mobile number against identity records. The verification happens during the Openpay account creation or first-purchase flow — customers who already have a verified Openpay account do not go through this step again.

Name mismatches are the most common cause. Customers who use a nickname, shortened name, or maiden name at checkout instead of their legal name will fail verification. A customer registered as "Katherine" at the credit bureau but using "Kate" at checkout will be declined. Hyphenated or double-barrelled surnames entered with or without hyphens are another frequent mismatch. The name entered during Openpay account creation must match identity documents exactly.

Address verification failures occur when the address entered during account creation doesn't match what is on record. Common causes: recent address change not yet reflected in identity records; rural or non-standard Australian address formats; PO box used instead of residential address (Openpay requires a residential street address); or unit/apartment number format mismatch (e.g., "Unit 4" vs "4/" vs "4/12").

Customers who fail identity verification cannot complete a new Openpay account and are locked out of using Openpay at any merchant until the issue is resolved. Openpay provides an alternative verification path (ID document upload) for customers who cannot pass the automated check — this is done through the Openpay app or website, not at the merchant's checkout.

Fix Steps for Customers

  1. Use your legal name exactly: Enter your full legal first name and surname as they appear on your passport or driver's licence — no nicknames, shortened forms, or initials
  2. Use your current residential address: Enter your street address (not a PO box) in the format used in credit bureau records — check your bank statement or utility bill for the exact format your address appears in
  3. Verify your date of birth is correct: A single digit transposition in the date or year will cause a mismatch — double-check before submitting
  4. Confirm your mobile number: The mobile number must be registered in your name — a prepaid or shared number may not be verifiable
  5. Confirm you are 18 or older: Openpay requires all customers to be at least 18 years of age
  6. Use the Openpay manual verification path: If automated verification fails, Openpay offers an alternative path to verify via government ID document upload — access this through the Openpay app or openpay.com
  7. Use an alternative payment method: While awaiting manual verification, use Afterpay, Zip, or a standard card payment to complete your purchase
Merchant note: Identity verification failures are entirely between the customer and Openpay. You cannot verify customers on their behalf. If customers are consistently reporting Openpay account creation failures at your checkout, consider adding a pre-checkout note directing new Openpay customers to create their account at openpay.com before checkout — reducing mid-checkout friction.
4

Establishment Fee Confusion Causing Checkout Abandonment

Merchant + Customer

Openpay's per-purchase establishment fee is the most commercially significant feature that distinguishes it from competing BNPL products — and the one most frequently causing unexpected checkout abandonment. Unlike Afterpay, Laybuy, or Zip Pay (which are marketed as zero-fee products where only late payments incur fees), Openpay charges customers an establishment fee when they start a new payment plan.

How the fee appears: When a customer selects Openpay at checkout and reaches the plan selection screen, they see a breakdown of their payment plan: the purchase amount, the establishment fee added to the plan, and the resulting installment amounts. For a customer who arrived at checkout expecting to split their AU$400 order into four equal AU$100 fortnightly payments with no additional cost, seeing an establishment fee — even a modest one — can cause immediate abandonment. The customer perceives the fee as a hidden charge that wasn't communicated before checkout.

Fee variation by plan duration: Openpay offers multiple plan durations, and the establishment fee typically increases with longer repayment periods. A 2-month plan carries a lower fee than a 12-month plan for the same purchase amount. Customers are sometimes shown multiple plan options with different fee structures and abandon because comparing plans mid-checkout is cognitively heavy. The plan selection screen is the highest-dropout point in the Openpay checkout flow.

Merchants who add Openpay without explicitly communicating the establishment fee on product pages, in the cart, or in pre-checkout messaging consistently see lower conversion rates for Openpay than for fee-free BNPL alternatives. This is not a technical failure — it is a messaging and expectation-setting failure that merchants can address.

Fix Steps for Merchants

  1. Add establishment fee disclosure on product pages: Below the Openpay instalment messaging widget, add a line: "Plans include an establishment fee — see plan options at checkout" — sets expectations before the checkout screen
  2. Update cart page messaging: If you display Openpay payment options in the cart (via the Openpay widget), ensure the widget version you are using shows the establishment fee, not just the installment amount
  3. Review your plan options: Log into the Openpay merchant portal and review which plan durations are offered at your store — if you offer longer-term plans with higher fees, consider whether customers whose AOV doesn't warrant a long plan should be offered shorter, lower-fee alternatives
  4. Compare abandonment against other BNPL: Use Shopify analytics to compare checkout initiation vs completion rates for Openpay vs Afterpay or Zip — a significantly lower Openpay completion rate is a signal of establishment fee surprise rather than a technical issue
  5. Consider promoting for high-AOV purchases only: Position Openpay explicitly as the right choice for larger purchases where spreading over many months is genuinely useful — for sub-AU$200 carts, a fee-free 4-payment product may convert better
  6. Contact Openpay to review plan fee structures: Openpay merchant agreements vary — if your establishment fees are higher than typical for your industry, contact Openpay merchant support to review your plan terms
Industry context: Openpay's establishment fee model is standard in sectors like healthcare and automotive where purchase values are high and credit products traditionally carry arrangement fees. Customers buying a AU$3,000 dental procedure are more fee-tolerant than customers buying a AU$120 fashion item. Openpay converts best when positioned appropriately for the purchase context.
5

Repayment or Direct Debit Payment Failed Post-Checkout

Customer-side

Openpay collects scheduled repayments via direct debit from the customer's nominated bank account or via recurring card charge, depending on the account configuration. Post-checkout repayment failures can occur weeks or months after the original purchase — which is particularly relevant for longer Openpay plans that span multiple months.

Bank account changes are the leading cause of mid-plan failures. A customer who set up Openpay six months ago with a bank account that has since been closed, consolidated into another account, or transferred to a new bank will have all subsequent direct debits fail. Unlike a card replacement (which is usually prompted by expiry), bank account changes often happen without the customer immediately noticing the impact on existing direct debit arrangements. The Openpay direct debit continues to target the old account details until the customer updates them in the Openpay app.

Insufficient funds on debit day is the most common transactional cause. Openpay processes scheduled debits at a specific time on the agreed payment dates. If the nominated account has insufficient funds at debit time, the transaction fails. Openpay retries over subsequent days with SMS and email notifications. Customers who don't notice or respond to these notifications will accumulate an overdue balance leading to account restriction.

For longer Openpay plans — spanning 6, 12, or 24 months — the probability of a mid-plan payment method change (bank account closure, card replacement after loss/fraud) is significantly higher than for Afterpay's 8-week fixed cycle or Laybuy's 6-week cycle. This is inherent to Openpay's longer repayment horizon and creates more post-checkout support contacts than shorter-duration BNPL products.

Fix Steps for Customers

  1. Check the Openpay app immediately: Failed payments appear prominently in the Openpay app with a "Pay now" or "Update payment details" prompt — open the app when you receive an SMS or email notification
  2. Update bank account details: If you changed banks, closed an account, or transferred to a new transaction account since setting up Openpay, go to Openpay app → Account → Payment Methods → update your direct debit bank account (BSB and account number)
  3. Ensure funds are available before scheduled debits: Check your Openpay plan schedule in the app — ensure the nominated bank account has sufficient funds the evening before each scheduled debit date
  4. Update card details if repaying by card: If your account uses card repayment, go to Openpay app → Account → Payment Methods → update to a current card with sufficient credit limit
  5. Contact your bank if debits are being blocked: Some banks block new direct debit authorities automatically — contact your bank to confirm Openpay's direct debit is not being blocked and whitelist it if necessary
  6. Pay overdue amounts manually before the next scheduled debit: Use the "Pay now" option in the Openpay app with an alternative payment method to clear overdue amounts before the next debit attempt — this prevents late fees and account restriction
Merchant note: Openpay pays merchants upfront at the time of purchase — your revenue is not affected by whether the customer successfully repays their installments. However, customers experiencing payment failures may contact your store first, especially if they are confused about the Openpay relationship. Direct all repayment and account questions to Openpay customer support — merchants do not have access to customer account or repayment data.
6

Customer Account Restricted — Overdue Balance

Customer-side

When an Openpay customer's scheduled repayment fails and all retry attempts are exhausted without resolution, Openpay restricts the account. A restricted Openpay account cannot be used for new purchases at any Openpay-accepting merchant — including your Shopify store — until the total overdue balance is paid in full. This is the most common reason a returning Openpay customer who has successfully used Openpay before cannot complete a new checkout.

The restriction lifecycle: Openpay attempts the scheduled debit or card charge → payment fails → Openpay sends SMS and email notification → Openpay retries over several days → all retries fail → Openpay issues overdue notices → account is restricted and blocked from new purchases. Customers who miss or dismiss the initial failure notifications may be unaware they are restricted until they next attempt an Openpay checkout — which may be weeks later.

Late payment fees accumulate on the overdue balance — the amount shown in the Openpay app at restriction will be higher than the original missed payment amount. Customers must pay the total overdue amount, including fees, to have account access restored. For customers with multiple active Openpay plans (Openpay can support several concurrent plans with different merchants), a payment failure on one plan restricts the entire account across all active plans and all future new purchases.

Fix Steps for Customers

  1. Open the Openpay app: The home screen shows overdue balances and account restrictions with a "Pay now" or "Resolve" option
  2. Pay the total overdue balance: Use an alternative payment method (different bank account or card) to clear the full overdue amount — partial payment may not restore account access
  3. Include late fees in the payment: The overdue total in the Openpay app includes all accumulated late fees — pay the amount shown, not just the original missed installment
  4. Wait for account reinstatement: After payment confirms, Openpay typically reinstates accounts within a few hours — if access is not restored within 24 hours, contact Openpay customer support with the payment confirmation reference
  5. Update payment details after reinstatement: Once reinstated, immediately update your primary bank account or card to the current, funded account to prevent a recurrence on the next scheduled payment
  6. Set up advance payment reminders: Add each Openpay scheduled payment date to your calendar — ensure funds are in the nominated account the night before each debit date to avoid the failure-retry-restriction cycle
Merchant note: Account restriction appears as a generic Openpay decline at checkout — you will not see the reason. If a customer says Openpay worked previously but is now failing, an overdue balance restriction is the most likely explanation. Direct them to check the Openpay app. Your store's revenue from their prior Openpay orders is secure — Openpay paid you upfront.
7

Unsupported Country or Currency (AUD / NZD Only)

Merchant-side

Openpay operates primarily in Australia (AUD) and New Zealand (NZD). Openpay will not appear at Shopify checkout unless the active checkout currency is AUD or NZD and the customer's shipping address is in a supported country. Both conditions must be true simultaneously — an Australian shipping address with a USD checkout currency will suppress Openpay, and an AUD currency store with a US shipping address will also suppress it.

Multi-currency Shopify stores are the most common source of this failure. A store based in Australia that added international shipping and configured Shopify Markets may have inadvertently changed how currencies are presented. If the store now operates with USD as the base currency and displays AUD as a currency conversion for Australian customers, Openpay's currency detection will not trigger — it requires AUD to be the actual checkout transaction currency, not a display conversion. The fix is to configure Shopify Markets with a fixed AUD currency for the Australian market, not a conversion.

Merchants who recently migrated from a domestic-only Shopify plan to a multi-market plan should specifically audit their Openpay checkout behaviour after the migration. International payment configuration changes frequently suppress domestic alternative payment methods that were working before the expansion.

Fix for Merchants

  1. Set per-market fixed currencies: Shopify Admin → Settings → Markets → Australia → confirm AUD is the fixed checkout currency — not a runtime conversion from a USD base; repeat for New Zealand with NZD
  2. Scope Openpay per market: In Openpay app settings and Shopify payment settings, confirm Openpay is enabled for the Australian and New Zealand markets — not as a global alternative payment method applied to all markets
  3. Test each market end-to-end: Add a cart, enter an AU shipping address with AUD currency → confirm Openpay appears; repeat for NZ with NZD currency independently
  4. Audit currency conversion settings: Confirm that AUD and NZD checkout currencies are fixed to regional currencies for their respective markets, not runtime conversions from another base
  5. Check Shopify Markets currency locking: Shopify Admin → Markets → Australia → Currencies → confirm "customers can only pay in AUD" is enabled, not optional multi-currency selection
  6. Re-test after any international expansion: Whenever you add a new market or change global currency settings, re-test Openpay specifically for AU and NZ customers — international expansion is the most common trigger for domestic alternative payment regression
US market note: Openpay previously launched operations in the United States but wound down the US service. Shopify merchants who configured Openpay for US customers will find it no longer available there. If US customers are reporting Openpay checkout failures, the service is not operational in that market — offer Afterpay, Klarna, or Affirm as US-compatible alternatives.
