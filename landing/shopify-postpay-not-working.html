Shopify Postpay Not Working — Not Showing, Declined, Identity Verification Failed

Updated June 2026 · 7 failure modes · UAE, Saudi Arabia & Egypt merchants

Postpay is a UAE-founded BNPL platform acquired by Zip in 2021, operating across UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Egypt with a Pay-in-4 model. It is the only major MENA BNPL that covers all three markets under one Shopify integration — but it fails in ways that reflect both its MENA-specific identity requirements and the post-acquisition integration complexity that Zip's ownership introduced. This guide covers all 7 Postpay failure modes with exact diagnostic steps for UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Egypt merchants on Shopify.

How Postpay Differs From Tabby and Tamara

Postpay is the only MENA BNPL that covers UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Egypt under one Shopify integration. Tamara covers Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, and Bahrain — but not Egypt. Tabby covers UAE, Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Kuwait, and Qatar — the closest competitor. Postpay's three-market footprint (UAE + KSA + Egypt) makes it the single most useful BNPL integration for merchants whose customer base spans both the GCC and North Africa. Merchants who only need GCC coverage often combine Tamara and Tabby; merchants who specifically need Egypt plus UAE/KSA may find Postpay sufficient as a standalone integration.

Postpay uses a Pay-in-4 model — four equal monthly installments, first payment at checkout. This is structurally similar to Tabby's Pay-in-4 and different from Tamara's Pay-in-3 (one-third at 0, 30, and 60 days). Postpay's installment amounts are equal quarters of the cart total. The Postpay widget displays the per-installment amount on product pages and at checkout — configure the widget's minimum display price to match the market minimum (100 AED / 100 SAR / varies EGP) to avoid showing the widget on products that won't qualify.

Zip's acquisition created branding complexity that some Shopify merchants encounter. Zip acquired Postpay in 2021 and operates it as a regional brand under the Zip portfolio alongside Zip US (formerly Quadpay) and Zip Australia. Most MENA merchants see the Postpay brand at checkout — but some merchant portal views, API documentation, or checkout widget configurations may reference "Zip" or show Zip co-branding. If your checkout shows unexpected Zip branding or your API calls return responses referencing Zip endpoints, this is a post-acquisition infrastructure artifact. Merchants who integrated Postpay before 2022 and have not updated their app version are most likely to encounter outdated configurations.

Postpay shows customers their available spending power in the app, similar to Tamara. Unlike Tabby's opaque limit model (where customers discover the limit only when declined), Postpay shows customers their available Postpay balance in the app. This means limit-declined customers can self-diagnose the issue — but they still contact merchants asking why Postpay "didn't work." Merchants should include a brief note directing these customers to the Postpay app to check their balance and pay down open plans before retrying.

1

Postpay Not Showing at Shopify Checkout

Merchant-side

Postpay requires the same two-step activation that affects all third-party BNPL apps on Shopify. Installing the Postpay app from the Shopify App Store is step one — but Postpay must also be manually activated as an alternative payment provider in Shopify Admin → Settings → Payments → Alternative payment providers. These two steps are independent and the second is frequently missed. Merchants who install the app and proceed to test checkout are often confused when Postpay doesn't appear, because the app is installed and active — but not enabled at the payment layer.

Currency is the primary suppressor for multi-market stores. Postpay appears at checkout only when the active transaction currency is AED (UAE), SAR (Saudi Arabia), or EGP (Egypt). Stores pricing globally in USD with auto-conversion to local currencies for display will not show Postpay — the transaction must settle in AED, SAR, or EGP, not in USD displayed as local currency. This is the most common cause of Postpay disappearing after a merchant expands internationally using Shopify Markets.

Additional causes of Postpay not showing: the merchant's Postpay account onboarding is incomplete or pending document review — Postpay requires business registration verification and may take 2–5 business days to approve merchant accounts in UAE, with slightly longer timelines for Saudi Arabia and Egypt; test or sandbox API credentials are configured in the Postpay app instead of live production keys; the Postpay app has not been updated since Zip's acquisition and may be using deprecated API endpoints; or the order contains products in categories that Postpay does not support (digital goods, gift cards, financial services).

Per-market activation is required in the Postpay merchant portal. UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Egypt are activated separately in the Postpay merchant dashboard. Enabling Postpay for UAE does not automatically enable it for Saudi Arabia or Egypt. Merchants who expand to new markets must separately activate each market in the Postpay portal — a common gap for merchants who set up Postpay for their primary UAE market and later add Saudi Arabia or Egypt sales without revisiting the Postpay portal.

How to Diagnose

  1. Check the two-step activation: Shopify Admin → Apps → confirm the Postpay app is installed; then Shopify Admin → Settings → Payments → Alternative payment providers → confirm Postpay is listed and enabled. Installation alone does not enable Postpay at checkout.
  2. Check store currency per market: Shopify Admin → Settings → Markets → UAE → confirm AED is the fixed checkout currency (not a USD auto-conversion); repeat for Saudi Arabia (SAR) and Egypt (EGP)
  3. Check Postpay merchant account status per market: Log into the Postpay merchant portal and confirm your account is fully approved and active for each market — UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Egypt are independently activated; accounts pending document review cannot process transactions
  4. Check API credential mode: In the Postpay app settings, confirm Live (production) API public and secret keys are configured — sandbox/test credentials suppress Postpay at live checkout; this is a common oversight left over from initial integration testing
  5. Update the Postpay app: Check the Shopify App Store for Postpay app updates — post-Zip-acquisition, merchants who have not updated may be running deprecated API configurations; the updated app version connects to the current Zip/Postpay infrastructure
  6. Test cart above minimum: Add physical products totalling above the minimum for each market (100 AED / 100 SAR / varies EGP) and test checkout with a supported shipping address — Postpay appears for valid carts above the minimum
  7. Test in incognito with a supported shipping address: Open a private browser, add products above the minimum, enter a UAE, Saudi Arabia, or Egypt shipping address, and proceed to checkout — rules out browser cache and session state
Per-market activation tip: After Zip's acquisition of Postpay, the merchant portal may show both Postpay and Zip branding in different sections. The market activation toggles are typically in the Postpay section — confirm each of UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Egypt is individually active, regardless of whether the portal shows "Postpay" or "Zip" labeling for the toggle.
2

Zip Acquisition Integration Confusion

Merchant-side

Zip acquired Postpay in 2021 as part of its MENA expansion strategy. In the MENA region, Postpay retains its brand identity — customers see "Postpay" at checkout and in the app. However, the underlying infrastructure, API layer, and merchant portal have progressively integrated into Zip's global platform. This creates a set of post-acquisition integration issues that are unique to Postpay and do not appear with Tamara, Tabby, or other MENA BNPL providers.

The most common post-acquisition issue: checkout shows "Zip" branding instead of "Postpay." Merchants who integrated Postpay before the acquisition or who have outdated app configurations may find their checkout displaying "Zip Pay" or Zip's logo instead of Postpay branding. This causes immediate customer confusion — customers expecting Postpay as a MENA payment option do not recognize Zip, and may abandon or contact merchant support. The fix is to ensure you are using the current Postpay Shopify app version, which routes through the correct regional branding configuration.

API endpoint changes post-acquisition may affect merchants using custom integrations. Merchants who built custom Postpay integrations (rather than using the standard Shopify app) may find that some API endpoints changed or were deprecated after the Zip acquisition. If you have a custom integration and Postpay is intermittently failing or returning unexpected responses, review the current Postpay/Zip developer documentation for updated API specs. The standard Shopify app handles endpoint updates automatically on app version updates.

A related confusion: Zip operates multiple BNPL products globally — Zip US (formerly Quadpay), Zip AU, Zip UK, and Postpay (MENA). If a merchant uses Zip in another market (e.g., Zip US for American customers via a separate Shopify integration), the Postpay integration is a different product and a separate merchant account. Do not attempt to use Zip US or Zip AU credentials for the MENA Postpay integration — they are separate systems.

How to Resolve

  1. Update the Postpay Shopify app to the latest version: Shopify Admin → Apps → Postpay → check for updates; the current version should route branding correctly and use the post-acquisition API infrastructure
  2. Verify branding in a test checkout: After updating, complete a test checkout and confirm the payment method displays as "Postpay" (not "Zip" or "Zip Pay") — if Zip branding persists, contact Postpay merchant support
  3. Confirm API credentials are from the Postpay merchant portal (not Zip AU or Zip US): Your Postpay merchant account credentials are issued from the regional MENA Postpay merchant portal — using credentials from Zip's other regional portals will fail
  4. Contact Postpay merchant support for custom integrations: If you have a custom API integration, request the current API documentation from Postpay merchant support — do not rely on pre-acquisition API documentation, as some endpoints and authentication patterns changed after the Zip integration
  5. Check webhook configurations: Post-acquisition, some webhook endpoints changed; if order confirmation, refund, or payment status webhooks stopped working, request updated webhook URLs from Postpay merchant support
Merchant note: If your checkout is showing "Zip" branding to MENA customers, this is a visible trust signal problem — MENA customers know Postpay but may not recognize the Zip brand. Fix the branding issue promptly by updating the Shopify app and confirming with Postpay support that your account is on the MENA regional configuration. Do not assume the issue will self-resolve; it requires an active fix.

Per-Purchase Spending Limit Check Failed — "Not Available for This Order"

Customer-side

Postpay evaluates each purchase against the customer's current available spending power — a real-time check of their Postpay account balance, outstanding plan obligations, and credit profile. This is not a one-time account approval: every checkout triggers an independent decision. A customer who successfully used Postpay last week may be declined today if their available spending power has been consumed by other open Postpay plans.

Postpay shows customers their available balance in the app — similar to Tamara, unlike Tabby. Tabby's spending limit is opaque; customers discover it only through a decline. Postpay takes a more transparent approach: the Postpay app shows customers their available Postpay spending power. When a customer is declined because their limit is insufficient, they can open the Postpay app, see their available balance, understand the shortfall, and take action (paying down open plans to free spending power) before retrying. However, many customers still contact merchants asking why Postpay "rejected" their order — they may not immediately connect the app balance to the checkout decline.

New Postpay customers face more conservative initial limits. First-time Postpay users receive a spending power allocation calibrated to their initial risk profile, which is typically more conservative than what an established Postpay user with a solid payment history receives. Customers who have never used Postpay and try to make a large first purchase may be approved for a partial amount or declined entirely. This is by design — Postpay builds customer credit profiles over time. New users who build a track record of on-time plan completions see their spending power increase.

Merchants cannot access individual customer decline reasons or appeal decisions on a customer's behalf. The decline decision is between Postpay and the customer. Best practice: make Tabby, Tamara, or credit card payment visibly available at checkout alongside Postpay — this provides a fallback when Postpay declines, reducing abandonment.

Fix Steps for Customers

  1. Check your available Postpay balance in the app: Open the Postpay app → Home or My Account → view your current available spending power; if it is less than your cart total, you cannot proceed with this purchase until you free up balance
  2. Pay down open Postpay plans: Postpay app → My Plans → review active plans → make an early payment on any open plan to reduce your outstanding balance and increase your available spending power
  3. Try a lower cart value: If your available spending power partially covers the cart, reducing the cart value below your remaining available amount may allow the transaction to proceed
  4. Wait for an open plan to complete: As existing Postpay plans finish paying off, your spending power is restored — retry the purchase once a plan has completed
  5. Contact Postpay customer support: If you believe the decline is an error or if you want to understand what action would increase your available spending power, Postpay support via the app is typically faster than email
  6. Use Tabby, Tamara, or a credit card as an alternative: If Postpay spending power is temporarily insufficient, Tabby covers UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Egypt as a direct MENA BNPL alternative; use a credit card and retry Postpay once your balance refreshes
Merchant note: "Postpay not available for this order" messages are almost always customer-level spending limit decisions, not a problem with your Postpay integration. If some customers see this error while others check out with Postpay successfully, your integration is working. If all customers see the error, check your merchant account status, per-market activation, and API credentials.

Identity Verification Failure — Emirates ID, Iqama, or Egypt National ID

Customer-side

Postpay requires identity verification during account creation and KYC. The document required varies by market: UAE customers verify with their Emirates ID; Saudi Arabia nationals and expatriate residents verify with their National ID (Huwiyya) or Iqama (residency permit); Egyptian customers verify with their Egypt National ID card. The verification is performed against government-linked data systems. Failures occur when the document is expired, the submitted data does not match records, or data propagation delays exist after recent document renewals.

UAE: Emirates ID verification is the most common failure point. Emirates ID is mandatory for Postpay verification in the UAE. Emirates ID verification fails when: the ID is expired (UAE requires periodic renewal — check the expiry date on the card); the name on the Emirates ID does not match what the customer entered (transliteration differences between Arabic and English versions of names cause mismatches — "Mohammed" vs "Mohamed" vs "Muhammad" are all valid but only one matches the Emirates ID record exactly); or the Emirates ID is newly issued after relocating to UAE and the data has not yet propagated to Postpay's verification partner (allow 24–72 hours after receiving a new Emirates ID). Customers who are new UAE residents still using a passport without an Emirates ID cannot complete full Postpay verification.

Saudi Arabia: National ID (Huwiyya) for citizens, Iqama for expatriate residents. Saudi national customers verify with their Huwiyya (National ID). Verification fails when the Huwiyya is expired or when the name entered does not match the ID exactly. Unlike Tamara (which uses Absher — the Saudi government digital identity platform — for a deeper integration), Postpay performs a more standard document verification check, which is somewhat less friction-intensive but also less authoritative. Saudi expatriates use their Iqama — verification fails if the Iqama is expired, the name transliteration differs from the document, or the Iqama data has not updated after a recent renewal or sponsor change.

Egypt: National ID card (Bithaqat al-Hawiyya) is required. Egyptian customers must provide their Egypt National ID. Verification fails when the ID is expired, the number is entered incorrectly (Egypt National ID is 14 digits — a common error is entering fewer digits or including spaces), or the personal data does not match government records. Egypt National ID verification is powered by Postpay's Egyptian data partnerships and has a slightly longer retry window than UAE or Saudi verification — if a customer fails and retries immediately, they should allow 15–30 minutes before the next attempt.

Fix Steps for Customers

  1. Check that your ID document is not expired: Emirates ID, Saudi Huwiyya/Iqama, and Egypt National ID all have expiry dates — an expired document will fail Postpay verification; renew your document before attempting Postpay verification
  2. Use your exact legal name as on the document: Enter your full legal name precisely as it appears on your Emirates ID, Huwiyya, Iqama, or Egypt National ID — including middle name if shown; avoid nicknames, abbreviated names, or alternate transliterations of Arabic names
  3. Enter the complete ID number without spaces or dashes: Emirates ID: 15 digits; Saudi National ID: 10 digits; Iqama: 10 digits; Egypt National ID: 14 digits — enter the full number continuously with no spaces or formatting characters
  4. Wait 72 hours after document renewal: If you recently renewed your Emirates ID, Huwiyya, Iqama, or Egypt National ID, allow 2–3 business days for updated data to propagate to Postpay's verification systems before retrying
  5. Allow 15–30 minutes between retry attempts: After a failed verification, Postpay's system may need a brief interval before accepting a new attempt — avoid retrying immediately; wait at least 15 minutes
  6. Contact Postpay support for manual review: If automated verification consistently fails despite a valid, current document, contact Postpay support via the app and request a manual document review — manual verification typically takes 1–3 business days
  7. Use an alternative payment method while verification resolves: Complete your purchase with a credit card, Apple Pay, or an alternative BNPL (Tabby or Tamara) while Postpay verification is pending — resolve Postpay identity verification in the app before your next purchase
Egypt-specific note: Postpay's Egypt coverage using local National ID verification makes it the only MENA BNPL that UAE-based merchants can use to serve Egyptian customers without a separate Tabby integration. However, the Egypt National ID verification pipeline is distinct from the UAE and Saudi pipelines — if UAE and Saudi verification works correctly but Egypt fails, it may indicate a market-specific issue with Postpay's Egyptian verification partner. Contact Postpay merchant support if Egypt verification consistently fails across multiple customers.

Phone Number Verification / OTP Failure

Customer-side

Postpay uses mobile phone number as the primary customer identifier and delivers one-time passwords (OTP) via SMS for account creation, verification, and checkout confirmation. Postpay's OTP infrastructure is calibrated for local SIM cards in its primary markets: UAE (+971, numbers starting 05x), Saudi Arabia (+966, numbers starting 05x), and Egypt (+20, numbers starting 01x). Customers providing phone numbers outside these formats encounter delivery failures or elevated friction.

Expatriates with home-country SIM cards face systematic OTP failures. UAE has an exceptionally high expatriate population — approximately 88% of UAE residents are expatriates, many of whom maintain home-country mobile numbers (Indian +91, Pakistani +92, Filipino +63, UK +44, US +1) as their primary phone. Postpay cannot reliably deliver OTP to these numbers through its UAE-calibrated SMS partnerships. A customer using a +91 Indian number in Dubai will typically fail Postpay OTP delivery or face significantly elevated friction. Acquiring a local UAE (+971) SIM is the structural fix — there is no workaround for consistently using a foreign number with Postpay.

Egyptian customers face additional OTP considerations. Egypt has specific carrier routing requirements for international SMS, and Postpay's Egyptian OTP delivery depends on local carrier partnerships. Egyptian customers on Vodafone Egypt, Etisalat Egypt (e&), and Orange Egypt should receive OTP reliably with a local +20 number. Customers using international roaming SIMs, VoIP numbers, or numbers registered in another country will face the same OTP failure pattern as UAE and Saudi expatriates with foreign SIMs.

Additional OTP failure causes: the OTP expires before the customer enters it — Postpay OTPs are short-lived (typically 2–5 minutes); the customer's carrier blocks SMS from international originating numbers (check carrier SMS spam filters); the customer is using a VoIP, virtual, or WhatsApp Business number rather than a registered personal mobile SIM; or SMS delivery is delayed during high-traffic periods such as Ramadan, White Friday, or National Day sales.

Fix Steps for Customers

  1. Use a local SIM card for your market: UAE: +971 05x number; Saudi Arabia: +966 05x number; Egypt: +20 01x number — a local registered personal mobile SIM is required for reliable Postpay OTP delivery in each market
  2. Confirm your number format includes the country code: UAE: +971 5X XXX XXXX; Saudi Arabia: +966 5X XXX XXXX; Egypt: +20 1X XXXX XXXX — ensure no typos and include the full international format
  3. Verify your number is not a VoIP or virtual number: WhatsApp Business numbers, VoIP services, and virtual phone numbers are not accepted for Postpay OTP — use a registered physical SIM card
  4. Request a new OTP immediately if the first expires: If you did not receive the OTP within 60 seconds or it has expired, tap Resend OTP before the verification session times out — enter the new code promptly
  5. Check carrier SMS filtering: Some GCC and Egyptian carriers filter SMS messages from international originating numbers — check your device's SMS spam folder and contact your carrier if Postpay OTP messages are not arriving
  6. Try a different device or SIM slot: If you have a dual-SIM device, confirm you are receiving SMS on the correct SIM that matches the number registered with Postpay
  7. Contact Postpay support: If a valid local number consistently fails OTP delivery, Postpay support may be able to assist with an alternative verification pathway or investigate a delivery issue with your specific carrier
Expat customer note: Postpay OTP is designed for locally-registered SIM cards in each market. This is a structural requirement of Postpay's phone-first identity model, not a temporary error. Expatriates using home-country numbers will typically face persistent OTP failures until they acquire a local SIM. This is the same constraint that affects Tamara and Tabby — all three MENA BNPL platforms require local SIM cards for reliable OTP delivery.

Order Value Below Postpay Minimum

Merchant + Customer

Postpay enforces minimum order thresholds in each market. If the cart value falls below the market minimum, Postpay does not appear at checkout — with no visible error message. The minimum exists because splitting a low-value order into four equal Pay-in-4 installments would produce installment amounts so small as to be impractical for both the customer and Postpay's payment infrastructure.

Market-specific minimums: UAE (approximately 100 AED), Saudi Arabia (approximately 100 SAR), Egypt (varies — typically in the range of 200–500 EGP depending on configuration). These thresholds apply to the cart subtotal before shipping costs. Shipping is not typically counted toward the minimum in standard configurations — confirm with Postpay merchant support if your specific configuration differs. Egyptian minimum amounts may vary and should be verified in your Postpay merchant portal, as Postpay's Egypt configuration has undergone updates since the Zip acquisition.

Merchants with product catalogs that include accessories, consumables, or lower-priced add-ons frequently encounter the minimum suppression issue. Postpay appears for main-product purchases but disappears for small replenishment or accessory orders. Customers who successfully used Postpay for a main purchase are then confused when Postpay is absent for a smaller follow-up purchase — they expect consistent availability across all orders.

Unlike some markets where BNPL minimums are rarely reached (Afterpay's AU$10 minimum), Postpay's UAE/Saudi minimum of 100 AED / 100 SAR is regularly hit for merchants selling beauty, personal care, or lifestyle accessories at the 30–80 AED/SAR price point. Configure the Postpay widget's minimum display price to match the market minimum — this prevents the widget from appearing on product pages that will not qualify at checkout.

Fix Steps for Merchants

  1. Test checkout with cart values above the minimum: If Postpay disappears for small orders, test with cart values above 100 AED (UAE), 100 SAR (Saudi Arabia), and the current EGP minimum (Egypt) — confirm Postpay reappears above the threshold
  2. Remove Postpay widgets from product pages below the minimum: Do not display "Pay in 4 with Postpay" widgets on product pages where the standalone price is below the market minimum — customers who see the widget expect Postpay to work at checkout
  3. Configure the widget minimum price: The Postpay Shopify widget has a minimum price configuration option — set this to match or slightly exceed the market minimum so the widget only appears on products that will qualify
  4. Add cart value guidance near the widget: Consider a message such as "Add AED X more to pay in 4 with Postpay" — this converts near-minimum carts by prompting small add-on purchases rather than losing the sale
  5. Bundle lower-priced items to cross the threshold: For products priced at AED 70–99 or SAR 70–99, consider bundle pricing or gift options that bring the cart above the Postpay minimum while adding genuine value
  6. Verify Egypt minimum in the Postpay merchant portal: The Egypt minimum may differ from the UAE and Saudi minimums and may have been updated post-acquisition — confirm the current EGP minimum in your Postpay merchant dashboard to configure the widget correctly
Widget configuration tip: Set the minimum display price on both the Postpay product page widget and the cart widget. If either widget shows Postpay for products below the minimum, customers who click through to checkout will encounter a confusing absence of the payment option they saw on the product page — this is the most common source of "Postpay was advertised but not available at checkout" support tickets.

Store Currency or Shopify Markets Configuration Mismatch

Merchant-side

Postpay operates in AED (UAE), SAR (Saudi Arabia), and EGP (Egypt). It will not appear at checkout unless the active transaction currency is one of these three and the customer's shipping address is in the corresponding country. Both conditions must be true simultaneously — a UAE shipping address with a USD transaction currency will suppress Postpay, and an AED-denominated store that ships globally will suppress Postpay for non-UAE addresses.

Shopify Markets currency configuration is the most frequent source of this failure. Many UAE- or Saudi-based merchants started as single-country stores (AED or SAR base currency) and later expanded internationally using Shopify Markets. During expansion, merchants commonly set USD as the global base currency with local currencies presented as runtime display conversions. This satisfies international customers but breaks Postpay for all local customers — Postpay requires the actual transaction to settle in AED, SAR, or EGP, not a USD transaction displayed as local currency.

The Shopify Markets fix is per-market fixed checkout currencies: UAE market → fixed AED, Saudi Arabia market → fixed SAR, Egypt market → fixed EGP. A fixed checkout currency means Shopify processes the transaction in that local currency. A currency conversion or display-only setting processes the transaction in the store base currency (often USD) and only converts the display — which Postpay does not accept.

Egypt currency configuration requires separate attention. Egypt (EGP) currency setup in Shopify Markets is the same technically as UAE or Saudi, but EGP is less commonly configured by UAE-based merchants adding Egypt as a secondary market. A merchant who successfully configures AED and SAR as fixed currencies for UAE and Saudi markets may inadvertently leave Egypt on USD auto-convert when adding it as a new market. Postpay will then work for UAE and Saudi customers but not for Egyptian customers, even with Egypt activated in the Postpay portal.

Fix for Merchants

  1. Set per-market fixed currencies in Shopify Markets: Shopify Admin → Settings → Markets → UAE → set AED as the fixed checkout currency (not a conversion from USD); repeat for Saudi Arabia (SAR) and Egypt (EGP)
  2. Confirm "fixed" vs "auto-convert" setting: In each market's currency settings, confirm the currency type is "fixed" — not "auto-convert" or "display only" — Postpay is suppressed by auto-convert even when the display shows AED or SAR
  3. Check Egypt market separately: Egypt market currency configuration is independent of UAE and Saudi — confirm EGP is set as fixed checkout currency for the Egypt market, even if UAE and Saudi are already correctly configured
  4. Activate Postpay per market in the Postpay merchant portal: Confirm Postpay is active for each market (UAE, Saudi Arabia, Egypt) separately in the Postpay merchant dashboard — market-specific activation is required and independent of currency configuration
  5. Test each market independently: Test with a UAE shipping address + AED fixed currency; separately test with Saudi Arabia + SAR; separately test with Egypt + EGP — each market must be tested individually to confirm market-specific configurations work
  6. Re-test after any Shopify Markets changes: Whenever you add a new market, change the store base currency, or update currency settings, re-test Postpay for all three markets — currency changes can suppress MENA alternative payment methods without any visible error in Shopify Admin
USD-priced stores: Shopify stores that price globally in USD cannot offer Postpay to MENA customers. Postpay requires AED, SAR, or EGP as the transaction settlement currency. If your store sells in USD for all markets, Postpay will not appear regardless of app configuration. Offer credit card, Apple Pay, Tabby, or Tamara instead — and evaluate whether configuring fixed local currencies for MENA markets is justified by your MENA sales volume.
