Shopify Credit Card Not Working — Declined, Not Accepted, Card Errors (2026)

Credit card declining on your Shopify store? Some declines are merchant-configurable — card type not enabled, Radar AVS or CVV rules too aggressive, fraud score blocking legitimate orders. Others are issuer-side — expired card, insufficient funds, bank security block, 3D Secure failure. Here are all 8 failure modes with exact diagnosis steps and what you can actually fix.

Updated June 2026 8 failure modes Card Type Not Enabled · AVS Mismatch · CVV Failure · Radar Block · Expired Card · Bank Decline · 3D Secure · Transaction Limits

Credit card failures on Shopify split into two fundamentally different categories. The first is merchant-configurable declines: the card itself is perfectly valid and the customer has money, but Shopify's payment settings, fraud rules, or card type configuration are blocking the transaction. These you can fix. The second is issuer-side declines: Shopify's systems are working correctly, but the customer's bank or card network is rejecting the transaction for a reason outside your control.

The diagnostic starting point is the order record in Shopify Admin. When a credit card payment fails, Shopify records the failure reason on the order. Go to Shopify Admin → Orders, find the failed attempt (even declined orders may appear as abandoned checkouts or draft orders), and look for the payment status, fraud analysis section, and any decline codes. The decline reason tells you immediately which category you're dealing with.

First Check: Shopify Payments Fraud Settings
Most merchant-configurable credit card declines are controlled in one place: Shopify Admin → Settings → Payments → Shopify Payments → Manage → Fraud prevention. Check whether AVS and CVV rules are set to "Cancel order" (automatic decline) vs "Flag order for review" (manual review). If you're seeing legitimate customers declined, changing these from cancel to flag is the fastest fix. Do this before investigating individual failure modes.

Failure Mode 1: Card Type Not Enabled in Shopify Payments

Shopify Payments enables Visa and Mastercard by default. American Express, Discover, and UnionPay must be activated separately in your Shopify Payments settings. If these card types aren't enabled, customers holding Amex, Discover, or UnionPay cards receive a checkout error — the card entry form accepts their number but the payment fails silently or shows a "card not supported" message. This is one of the most common and easily missed causes of credit card failures on new stores.

Amex is particularly significant: in the United States, 20–30% of consumer spending is on American Express cards. If your store is losing Amex customers because the card type isn't enabled, you're losing a meaningful segment of sales with no error visible on the merchant side — just a high checkout abandonment rate from Amex cardholders.

How to enable American Express, Discover, and UnionPay

  1. Go to Shopify Admin → Settings → Payments
  2. Under Shopify Payments, click Manage
  3. Scroll to the Card types section
  4. Enable American Express and Discover if they're not already checked
  5. For international stores selling to China: enable UnionPay under the card types section
  6. Save changes and test with an Amex card number (use Shopify's test card numbers in test mode to verify)
Amex Processing Rates Are Higher — But Worth It
American Express charges merchants a slightly higher processing fee than Visa or Mastercard — typically 2.9%–3.5% depending on your Shopify plan and country, vs 2.4%–2.9% for Visa/Mastercard. This is why some merchants disable Amex. However, Amex cardholders tend to have higher average order values and lower chargeback rates. For most stores, enabling Amex increases net revenue despite the higher fee. Only disable Amex if your margins cannot absorb the rate difference on typical orders.

Failure Mode 2: AVS Mismatch Triggering a Radar Block

AVS (Address Verification Service) compares the billing address a customer enters at checkout against the address on file with their card issuer. When they don't match, the card network returns an AVS mismatch result. Shopify Payments' fraud prevention rules can be configured to automatically cancel orders with an AVS mismatch — and this is enabled by default on many stores.

AVS mismatches affect legitimate customers constantly: the customer moved and hasn't updated their bank address; they're using a work shipping address with a home billing address they mistype; international addresses format differently than US AVS expects; apartment numbers get dropped; "Street" vs "St" differs. An aggressive AVS cancel rule silently blocks real paying customers while showing them a generic "payment declined" error with no explanation of what went wrong or how to fix it.

Diagnosing AVS declines and adjusting Radar rules

  1. Go to Shopify Admin → Orders and look for any recently declined orders or orders marked with a fraud indicator
  2. Click an affected order and check the Fraud analysis section — look for "AVS street mismatch" or "AVS zip mismatch" indicators
  3. Go to Shopify Admin → Settings → Payments → Shopify Payments → Manage → Fraud prevention
  4. Find the AVS street address check and AVS postal/zip code check rules
  5. Change both rules from "Cancel order" to "Flag order for review" — this stops automatic cancellations while still alerting you to potential fraud
  6. For orders flagged for AVS mismatch: manually review the order for other fraud signals. If the order looks legitimate (matching IP country, reasonable customer details, prior purchase history), approve it manually in Shopify Admin
AVS Response Code Meaning Merchant Action Fraud Risk
Y — Full match Address and ZIP both match exactly Proceed — strong match Low
Z — ZIP match only ZIP matches; street address doesn't Review other fraud signals Medium
A — Address match only Street matches; ZIP doesn't Review other fraud signals Medium
N — No match Neither address nor ZIP matches Flag for review; don't auto-cancel Higher
U — Unavailable Card issuer doesn't support AVS (common internationally) Proceed — not a fraud signal Low
G — International Card issued outside US/Canada Proceed — not a fraud signal Low
International Customers Always Return AVS U or G
AVS was designed for US and Canadian cards. International customers — with UK, EU, Australian, Asian cards — will almost always return AVS code U (unavailable) or G (international/global). If your Shopify Payments fraud rule is set to cancel orders on AVS mismatch, you are blocking 100% of your international credit card customers. This is a common silent revenue leak for stores with any international traffic. Set AVS rules to "Flag for review" rather than "Cancel order."

Failure Mode 3: CVV Failure Blocking the Transaction

CVV (Card Verification Value) is the 3-digit code on the back of Visa and Mastercard cards, or 4-digit code on the front of Amex cards. Shopify Payments checks CVV against the card issuer's records. When CVV fails, it means the customer either entered the wrong security code, or — less commonly — the card issuer returned an unexpected CVV result that Shopify's fraud rules interpret as a mismatch.

Most CVV failures are genuine customer input errors — a typo in the 3-digit field, auto-fill populating the wrong value, or the customer using a virtual card number that doesn't carry a CVV. However, CVV failures are also used to automatically cancel orders in some Shopify Payments configurations, which can block legitimate transactions when the card issuer doesn't support CVV verification or returns a "not provided" response.

Diagnosing and handling CVV failures

  1. Check the failed order in Shopify Admin → Orders → Fraud analysis for "CVV mismatch" or "CVV check failed" indicators
  2. If you see CVV failures frequently across multiple customers: go to Shopify Admin → Settings → Payments → Shopify Payments → Fraud prevention and check the CVV check rule setting
  3. Change the CVV rule from "Cancel order" to "Flag order for review" if you're seeing legitimate orders blocked
  4. For individual customer CVV issues: ask the customer to re-enter their card number and CVV manually (not via auto-fill) — auto-fill can populate an old CVV from a replaced card
  5. If the customer uses a virtual card number (from a bank app or privacy service like Privacy.com): virtual cards sometimes don't carry a valid CVV for the underlying account — the customer needs to use the physical card or a different payment method
  6. Corporate credit cards and purchasing cards sometimes return "CVV not verified" rather than a match/mismatch — this is normal behavior for some B2B cards and shouldn't be treated as fraud
Never Log or Store CVV Values
CVV codes must never be stored, logged, or transmitted after authorization — this is a PCI DSS requirement. Do not add any logging or analytics that captures the CVV field value. Shopify Payments handles CVV verification internally and only returns a pass/fail result to your store. If you're using a custom checkout or headless architecture, verify that no CVV values are being persisted anywhere in your stack.

Failure Mode 4: Shopify Fraud Analysis Score Blocking the Order

Shopify Payments includes a built-in fraud analysis system that scores every order for risk. High-risk orders can be automatically cancelled before payment completes. The fraud score is calculated from dozens of signals: IP address location vs billing address country, email domain age, browser fingerprint, order velocity from the same IP, whether the shipping and billing addresses match, and more. When multiple signals align negatively, a legitimate customer can get blocked even with a perfectly valid card.

The most common false-positive fraud block scenarios: a customer using a VPN or corporate proxy appears to be in a different country than their billing address; a new customer making a first purchase with an email on a free provider (Gmail, Yahoo) combined with expedited shipping gets a high risk score; or a customer making an unusually large order compared to your store average gets flagged even though they're genuine. Each of these is a business decision about how aggressively to screen versus how many real customers to block.

Reviewing and managing high-risk order blocks

  1. When Shopify cancels or flags an order for fraud, go to Shopify Admin → Orders and click the flagged order
  2. Review the Fraud analysis section — Shopify shows which specific signals contributed to the high-risk assessment (e.g., "IP address not in same country as billing address," "Multiple orders from this IP")
  3. Evaluate whether the signals indicate actual fraud risk or a false positive: does the customer email look real? Is the billing address in a country that matches typical fraud patterns or just a VPN user?
  4. If you believe the order is legitimate: click Accept risk and process in the order — this manually overrides the fraud block and processes the payment
  5. If you're unsure: contact the customer by email to confirm order details before accepting — a real customer will respond; a fraudster will not
  6. To reduce false positives systematically: go to Shopify Admin → Settings → Payments → Shopify Payments → Fraud prevention and adjust which signals trigger automatic cancellation vs. flagging for review
Shopify Fraud Protection vs. Shopify Protect
There are two different fraud tools in Shopify: the built-in Fraud Analysis (available to all merchants) and Shopify Protect (previously called Shopify Fraud Protection — a paid add-on that covers chargeback losses on approved orders). The built-in Fraud Analysis flags risk but doesn't reimburse chargebacks. Shopify Protect guarantees reimbursement for orders it approves. If you're manually approving flagged orders, you're accepting the chargeback risk yourself. Evaluate whether Shopify Protect's coverage makes sense for your order volume and average order value.

Failure Mode 5: Expired Card

An expired credit card is one of the most self-explanatory declines but also one of the most frustrating for customers — especially when they don't realize their card has expired. Shopify's checkout validates the expiry date at entry (the form won't accept a date in the past), but expiry happens between when a customer last saved their card details and the current purchase. Customers with saved payment methods in Shop Pay or a logged-in account may have an expired card stored without realizing it.

Expired card declines are also common during the transition months of January and February when many cards expire at December 31 or January 31 of the prior year and replacement cards have been mailed but not activated or updated. Merchants selling subscription products or running saved-card recurring charges will see an uptick in expired card failures during these months.

Handling expired card failures at checkout

  1. An expired card decline shows in Shopify Admin with decline code 54 (expired card) or the message "Your card has expired" — this is a customer-side issue, not a merchant configuration problem
  2. If a customer contacts you about a payment failure: advise them to check their card expiry date and use a current card or an alternative payment method
  3. For customers using Shop Pay with a saved card: they need to log into their Shop Pay account and update their payment method to their new card before checkout
  4. For subscription merchants running recurring charges: implement a dunning flow that emails customers when their card is about to expire or when a charge fails due to expiry — most subscription apps (Recharge, Bold Subscriptions) have dunning features built in
  5. Consider adding Apple Pay, Google Pay, or Shop Pay as alternative payment methods — wallet-based payments auto-update when the underlying card is renewed and eliminate expired card failures entirely
Digital Wallets Eliminate Expired Card Problems
When a customer pays via Apple Pay, Google Pay, or Shop Pay, their underlying card details are stored in the wallet — and when the bank issues a new card, the wallet is automatically updated by the bank. The customer doesn't need to re-enter anything. For stores with high repeat-purchase rates or subscription products, getting customers to use digital wallet payments instead of manual card entry effectively eliminates expired card failures in your payment stack.

Failure Mode 6: Bank-Side Decline (Do Not Honor, Insufficient Funds)

Some credit card declines originate at the card issuer — the customer's bank — and have nothing to do with your Shopify configuration. These bank-side declines are outside merchant control. The two most common types are "insufficient funds" (the card doesn't have enough available credit or the account is maxed out) and "do not honor" (a generic issuer decline that covers everything from fraud prevention to account flags to the bank's own risk policies).

Bank-side declines are often temporary. A "do not honor" on the first attempt might succeed on a retry because the bank's fraud system flagged an unusual transaction that the customer can verify. Banks also frequently block the first international transaction until the customer calls to confirm — this is common for purchases from stores in other countries. As a merchant, you cannot override a bank-side decline; the customer must resolve it with their issuing bank.

Common bank-side decline codes and what to tell customers

Decline Code Meaning What to Tell the Customer
05 — Do Not Honor Generic bank decline; most common code Try again in a few minutes; if it repeats, contact your bank or use a different card
51 — Insufficient Funds Not enough available credit or funds Use a card with available credit or a different payment method
14 — Invalid Card Number Card number entered incorrectly Re-enter the card number carefully; check for typos
54 — Expired Card Card past its expiry date Use your current card; check the expiry date on your card
41 / 43 — Lost / Stolen Card Card reported to bank as lost or stolen Contact your card issuer — this card cannot be used
61 — Exceeds Withdrawal Limit Transaction exceeds card's daily limit Split payment across multiple methods or contact bank to raise limit
65 — Activity Limit Exceeded Too many transactions in a period Wait and retry; or use a different card
57 — Service Not Permitted Card type not authorized for this transaction type or merchant category Use a different card — this card's account settings restrict this purchase type
Show Customers Specific Guidance, Not Just "Try Again"
Shopify's default decline message "Your card was declined" gives customers no actionable information. Consider adding a checkout error message or post-decline email that tells customers specifically: "Your card was declined by your bank. Please try a different card, check that your card is not expired, or contact your bank if the problem persists. You can also complete your purchase with PayPal, Apple Pay, or Google Pay." A useful error message recovers sales; a generic one sends customers to a competitor.

Failure Mode 7: 3D Secure Authentication Abandoned or Failed

3D Secure (3DS) is an additional authentication layer that some card issuers require for online transactions. After the customer enters their card details, they're redirected to a bank authentication page or shown a popup asking them to verify their identity — through an SMS one-time password, a bank app approval, or a security question. If the customer closes this popup, doesn't receive the OTP, or takes too long to complete it, the transaction fails with a 3DS authentication failure.

3DS failures are increasing in frequency as regulatory requirements like PSD2 (Europe) and SCA (Strong Customer Authentication) push more banks to require step-up authentication. The failure can occur because: the customer received no OTP (SMS issues); the customer's browser blocked the popup; the authentication timed out (typically 10–15 minutes); or the customer simply wasn't expecting the extra step and closed it thinking it was an error.

Reducing 3DS abandonment at checkout

  1. A 3DS failure appears in Shopify Admin as "Authentication required" or "3D Secure authentication failed" in the order's payment status
  2. For customers who abandoned 3DS: send an abandoned checkout recovery email explaining what happened — "Your bank required an extra verification step during checkout. Please try again — you'll see a screen from your bank asking you to confirm the payment." Many customers will complete the purchase on retry once they know to expect the 3DS step
  3. Ensure your checkout's Content Security Policy (CSP) headers don't block 3DS redirect domains — some custom themes or security apps block the bank authentication popup iframe
  4. If your store uses Shopify Plus checkout customization: verify that no frame-src or child-src CSP directives are blocking *.visa.com, *.mastercard.com, or card issuer domains
  5. For European stores: 3DS is required under PSD2/SCA for most transactions — this isn't optional and 3DS failure recovery is critical for EU conversion rates
  6. Apple Pay and Google Pay bypass 3DS entirely — they use device-level biometric authentication (Face ID, Touch ID) which satisfies SCA requirements without the friction of a separate bank popup
Apple Pay and Google Pay Eliminate 3DS Friction
Wallet-based payments (Apple Pay, Google Pay, Shop Pay) authenticate using the device's biometric system — Face ID or Touch ID. This satisfies Strong Customer Authentication requirements without requiring a separate OTP or bank redirect. For stores with high rates of 3DS-related abandonment, prominently offering Apple Pay and Google Pay (especially on mobile where biometric authentication is seamless) directly reduces abandonment from authentication friction.

Failure Mode 8: Card Transaction or Daily Limit Exceeded

Card issuers impose per-transaction and daily spending limits on credit cards — limits the cardholder may not know exist until a transaction fails. These limits are especially relevant for high-average-order-value stores: electronics, furniture, luxury goods, or anything with orders over $500–$2,000. A customer's card might work perfectly for a $50 purchase but fail at $1,500 because it hits the card's single-transaction limit.

Business credit cards often have the opposite problem: very high limits, but restricted merchant category codes (MCC). A corporate card might decline purchases at merchants coded as "Entertainment" even though the actual item is a legitimate business purchase — the card's expense policy blocks the MCC, not the card itself. This is outside merchant control but worth knowing to guide customers toward an alternative payment method.

Helping customers work around transaction limits

  1. A transaction limit decline shows as decline code 61 (exceeds withdrawal limit) or 65 (activity limit exceeded) in Shopify order details
  2. Advise the customer to contact their bank to raise their per-transaction or daily limit — most banks can do this immediately over phone or app for cardholders with good account standing
  3. Offer split payment options if your store supports them — some customers can use two cards to split a high-value order
  4. For high-AOV stores: enable buy now, pay later options (Klarna, Afterpay, Affirm) — these route through the BNPL provider rather than the customer's card limit directly, effectively bypassing per-transaction limits for qualifying customers
  5. Bank wire transfer or direct bank debit (ACH) may be worth enabling for very large B2B orders — customers with high-value purchases often prefer or require this for corporate procurement
  6. Ensure your checkout clearly shows multiple payment methods so customers who hit a card limit can immediately switch to PayPal, Apple Pay, or another method without abandoning
Display Multiple Payment Options Prominently
For orders over $500, showing only a card entry form creates unnecessary friction and abandonment when cards hit limits or require 3DS. Display Apple Pay, Google Pay, PayPal, and BNPL options alongside card entry. Customers who hit a card limit should see an alternative immediately rather than having to hunt for it. Shopify's express checkout buttons (Apple Pay, Google Pay) appear automatically at the top of checkout — ensure they're not hidden by theme customizations.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is my Shopify store not accepting Amex or Discover cards?

American Express and Discover are not enabled by default in Shopify Payments. To accept them, go to Shopify Admin → Settings → Payments → Shopify Payments → Manage → Card types and enable American Express and Discover separately. Amex is particularly important if a significant portion of your customers are US-based — approximately 20–30% of US consumer credit spending is on American Express cards. After enabling, test with a real Amex card to confirm it processes.

Why does Shopify keep declining legitimate customer credit cards?

Shopify declining legitimate cards is almost always caused by fraud prevention settings that are too aggressive. The most common culprits: AVS (address verification) rule set to automatically cancel orders when billing address doesn't match — this blocks international customers and anyone who has moved. And CVV rules set to cancel on mismatch — some card types and corporate cards don't return a CVV match result even when the code is correct. Go to Shopify Admin → Settings → Payments → Shopify Payments → Fraud prevention and change both AVS and CVV rules from "Cancel order" to "Flag order for review." You'll still be alerted to potential fraud but won't lose legitimate sales to automatic cancellations.

What do I do when a customer's credit card keeps getting declined on Shopify?

When a specific customer's card keeps failing: first, check the order's fraud analysis in Shopify Admin for what triggered the decline. If it's AVS or CVV related, the customer can retry entering their billing address exactly as it appears on their bank statement. If it's a bank-side decline (codes 05, 51, 57), the customer needs to contact their card issuer or use a different payment method — you cannot override an issuer decline from the merchant side. Offer the customer alternative methods: PayPal, Apple Pay, Google Pay, or a buy now pay later option. A customer who wants to buy but can't use their card will convert if you make an alternative path easy.

Why does a credit card work on other sites but fail on my Shopify store?

A card working on other sites but failing specifically on your Shopify store points to your store's fraud prevention configuration, not the card itself. The most likely cause: your AVS or CVV fraud rules are configured more strictly than other merchants. Other merchants may accept AVS mismatches while yours cancels them. Check Shopify Admin → Settings → Payments → Shopify Payments → Fraud prevention and review what rules are set to "Cancel order" — loosen these to "Flag for review" and see if that specific card type of failure resolves.

Can I see why a credit card was declined on my Shopify store?

Yes — go to Shopify Admin → Orders, find the order or abandoned checkout, click it, and look at the payment status section and fraud analysis section. Shopify shows the decline type (AVS mismatch, CVV check failed, fraud score, or a bank decline code like "05" or "51"). The fraud analysis section shows which specific signals contributed to a high-risk assessment. Bank decline codes map to specific issuer reasons — see the decline code table in this guide for what each code means and how to advise the customer.
