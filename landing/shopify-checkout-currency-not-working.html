Shopify Checkout Currency Not Working — 8 Causes Explained

Currency switcher shows EUR but checkout charges USD? PayPal charging a different amount than Shopify displayed? iDEAL or Klarna disappeared after enabling multi-currency? Here are all 8 root causes for Shopify currency failures at checkout — for merchants using Shopify Markets, third-party gateways, and headless storefronts.

Updated June 2026 8 failure modes Shopify Markets · Multi-Currency Capture · PayPal · Exchange Rates · Local Payments · Headless

Shopify Markets introduced native multi-currency support — displaying prices in a customer's local currency, setting market-specific pricing, and (with Shopify Payments) capturing in the displayed currency. But "multi-currency" in Shopify is not a single switch: it's a stack of interdependent settings across payment gateways, market configurations, currency rounding rules, and Storefront API contexts. When any layer is misconfigured, customers see currency mismatches, unexpected charges, or missing payment methods.

The result: eight distinct failure modes that all look like "currency not working" but have completely different root causes. Most are architectural — they reflect how Shopify separates display currency from capture currency, and how third-party gateways handle the gap between the two.

Display Currency vs Capture Currency — The Core Distinction
Display currency: the currency shown to the customer on product pages, cart, and checkout — controlled by Shopify Markets and the storefront locale.

Capture currency: the currency in which the payment is actually charged and settled — controlled by the payment gateway's capabilities and configuration.

With Shopify Payments + multi-currency enabled: display and capture currency match — the customer pays in the displayed currency, Shopify settles in that currency.

With third-party gateways: Shopify displays in local currency but submits the charge in the store's primary currency (or the gateway's account currency). The customer's bank converts at its own rate and adds an FX fee. This is the single most common source of currency complaints.

Failure Mode 1: Displayed Currency ≠ Actual Charge — Third-Party Gateway Fallback

This is the most common currency complaint on multi-currency Shopify stores: the customer sees a price in EUR (or GBP, AUD, etc.), completes checkout, and then their bank charges them in USD at a different amount. Shopify didn't make an error — it displayed the correct local price, but the gateway captured in a different currency.

How multi-currency capture actually works

Payment Gateway Capture Currency Customer Charged In FX Fee?
Shopify Payments (multi-currency enabled) Displayed currency EUR, GBP, AUD, etc. — as shown No FX fee from Shopify — bank may still charge international transaction fee
Shopify Payments (multi-currency NOT enabled) Store primary currency Displayed currency is cosmetic — charge is in store's base Customer's bank adds FX fee (1-3%)
Third-party gateway (Stripe via direct integration) Store primary currency Bank converts from store's base currency Yes — gateway's currency conversion + bank FX fee
PayPal Express PayPal account currency Bank converts from merchant's PayPal account currency Yes — PayPal conversion fee (2.5-3.5%) + bank FX fee
Authorize.net, Braintree, Square Store primary currency Bank converts from store's base currency Yes — bank FX fee

How to enable Shopify Payments multi-currency capture

  1. Admin → Settings → Payments → Shopify Payments → Manage
  2. Under "Countries/Regions," confirm Shopify Payments is active for your store's country
  3. Admin → Markets → select the market (e.g., European Union) → Currency → set to EUR
  4. Shopify Payments automatically handles multi-currency capture for markets with an assigned currency — no additional toggle needed once the market currency is set
  5. Test: add a product to cart, switch country to Germany (use the country selector or append ?country=DE to the URL), verify that checkout shows EUR and confirms "You will be charged in EUR"
Multi-Currency Capture Requires Shopify Payments — Not Third-Party Gateways
Only Shopify Payments supports multi-currency capture natively. If you're using a third-party gateway as your primary payment method, the local currency display in Shopify Markets is cosmetic — all charges are submitted in your store's primary currency. To offer true local-currency checkout, you must either switch to Shopify Payments or use a third-party gateway that has its own multi-currency capture capability (configured entirely within that gateway, not within Shopify). Stripe, when configured as a direct Shopify payment method, does not support Shopify's multi-currency capture — it would need to be configured via Stripe's own presentment currency feature, which requires a custom integration.

Failure Mode 2: Currency Switcher Updates Product Prices But Checkout Reverts to Store Currency

A customer uses the storefront currency selector (built into the theme or added via an app) to switch from USD to GBP. Product prices update to show GBP amounts. They add to cart and proceed to checkout — but checkout shows USD prices. This is one of the most disorienting currency UX failures because Shopify showed GBP throughout the entire pre-checkout journey.

Why this happens

The checkout domain (checkout.shopify.com) picks up the cart's assigned presentment currency — not the storefront's displayed currency. For the checkout to show GBP, the cart's presentment currency must be set to GBP via Shopify's localization API at the time the cart is created or updated.

Currency Switcher Type Checkout Currency Why
Native Shopify Markets country/language selector (theme built-in) Correct local currency Uses /localization.json endpoint to set the cart's presentment currency; checkout inherits it
Third-party currency converter app (display-only) Store primary currency App uses JavaScript to multiply displayed prices but does not update the cart's presentment currency in Shopify; checkout ignores the JS transformation
Third-party app with Markets integration Correct local currency App calls /cart/update.json with correct presentment currency or uses Storefront API cartBuyerIdentityUpdate
URL-based market switching (?country=GB) Correct local currency Shopify's Markets routing sets market context including currency when the URL parameter is present

How to diagnose which switcher type you have

  1. Open browser developer tools → Network tab → filter for "localization" or "cart"
  2. Switch the currency on the storefront and watch for network requests
  3. If you see a POST to /localization or a Storefront API call to cartBuyerIdentityUpdate, the switcher is updating the cart — currency should carry through to checkout
  4. If you only see JavaScript activity with no network requests to Shopify's API, the switcher is display-only and will revert at checkout
  5. Check the response of /cart.json (open in a new tab after switching currency) — look for "currency" field; if it shows the store's primary currency after switching, the switcher is display-only
Using Shopify's Native Market Selector vs Currency-Only Apps
Shopify's built-in country/currency selector (added via theme customizer → sections → Country/region selector) is the recommended approach for Shopify Markets. It sets both the country context and the currency simultaneously, ensuring local payment methods, tax rules, and shipping rates also reflect the correct market. Currency-only converter apps that only change displayed prices are a workaround from before Shopify Markets existed — they don't integrate properly with the Markets system and will cause checkout currency mismatches.

Failure Mode 3: PayPal Express Charges a Different Amount Than Shopify Displayed

PayPal Express Checkout is a separate payment flow that bypasses Shopify's standard checkout currency handling. When a customer taps the PayPal button, they're handed off directly to PayPal's payment screen — and PayPal uses its own currency logic, not Shopify's Markets currency.

How PayPal handles multi-currency

PayPal captures payment in the currency supported by the merchant's PayPal business account. Shopify passes the order total to PayPal, but if the PayPal account's primary currency is USD and the customer's cart was displayed in EUR, PayPal converts EUR → USD at its own exchange rate (which differs from the rate Shopify Markets used to display the EUR price). PayPal adds a currency conversion fee of 2.5-3.5% for this conversion. The customer's bank may also add a foreign transaction fee on the USD charge if the customer's card is issued in a non-USD country.

Scenario What Shopify Showed What PayPal Charged Difference
EUR customer, USD merchant PayPal account €85.00 EUR $93.40 USD → converted back by bank at their rate €2-4 difference from PayPal conversion fee + bank FX fee
GBP customer, USD merchant PayPal account £72.00 GBP $91.20 USD → bank converts to GBP at bank rate £1-3 difference from conversion fees
AUD customer, AUD merchant PayPal account A$140.00 AUD A$140.00 AUD No difference — currencies match

How to prevent PayPal currency discrepancies

  • Add multiple PayPal account currencies: In your PayPal business account, add EUR, GBP, AUD, etc. as secondary currencies — PayPal will then capture in those currencies without conversion when the order matches
  • Enable PayPal's multi-currency feature: Available in PayPal business account settings under "Selling Tools" → "Block payments" → configure currency handling
  • Switch to Shopify Payments as primary: The cleanest solution — remove PayPal as a payment option for markets where currency mismatch occurs, or only show PayPal for customers whose currency matches your PayPal account currency
  • Disclose the conversion in checkout: Add a note near the PayPal button explaining that PayPal may convert currency at its own rate
PayPal Currency Mismatch Can Cause Silent Capture Failures on Multi-Currency Stores
On some multi-currency Shopify configurations, if PayPal cannot process the cart's currency at all (not just a conversion difference, but an unsupported currency for the merchant's PayPal account), PayPal silently fails to capture the order. Shopify shows the order as completed but no payment is received. Merchants don't always receive an alert. If you see orders in "Payment pending" status that used PayPal Express on a multi-currency store, check the order timeline for "Payment failed" events and cross-reference against your PayPal transaction history.

Failure Mode 4: Price at Checkout Differs Slightly From Product Page — Exchange Rate Staleness

A customer adds a product to their cart when it's priced at €84.99. By the time they reach the checkout payment step 15 minutes later, the checkout shows €85.49. Neither figure is wrong — but customers (and merchants) don't expect prices to change mid-session.

Why Shopify exchange rates drift during a session

Shopify Markets uses exchange rates that refresh on a schedule — not in real time. The exact refresh frequency varies but is typically every few hours. When the rate updates between a customer's product browsing and their checkout, the checkout price reflects the new rate while the product page price they saw earlier reflected the old rate. The discrepancy is typically small (under 1%) but it's enough to trigger customer complaints.

Rate Source Update Frequency Discrepancy Risk
Shopify auto-rate (Markets managed rate) Every few hours Low but present — small rate movements mid-session
Fixed price list per market (manual) Never — merchant controls it None — price is exact, no conversion math
Third-party currency converter app Varies (some update every few minutes) Higher — app rate and Shopify checkout rate may differ significantly

Eliminating rate discrepancies with fixed market prices

  1. Admin → Markets → select a market (e.g., European Union) → Products → Price adjustments
  2. Select "Manual" and set a fixed percentage markup or use a price list to specify exact prices per product
  3. With fixed prices, Shopify does not apply exchange rate math at all — the customer always sees and pays the exact price you specified
  4. This is especially recommended for markets where you run promotions or have price-sensitive customers who may notice even small discrepancies
  5. Note: fixed prices must be maintained manually when product prices change — Shopify does not auto-update fixed market prices when the base price changes
Rounding Rules Also Affect the Final Amount
Shopify Markets applies rounding rules to converted prices to make them look natural in the target market. A €84.732 conversion might round to €84.99 (nearest .99 ending) rather than €84.73. You can configure rounding rules per market in Admin → Markets → [Market] → Price adjustments → Rounding. The default rounding is "No rounding," which can produce psychologically unappealing price endings. Merchants often set .99 or .00 endings — but this means the displayed price is not the mathematically exact conversion, which can confuse customers who try to verify the rate themselves.

Failure Mode 5: Multi-Currency Rounding Produces a Different Amount Than the Quoted Price

A customer in Germany sees a price of €49.99 in Shopify's checkout. Their bank statement shows a charge of €50.12. The extra €0.13 is not fraud, a fee, or an error — it's the difference between Shopify's rounded display price (€49.99) and the actual converted amount at the exact exchange rate applied at capture time. This is especially confusing when the customer expected to pay exactly what Shopify showed.

The gap between display rounding and actual capture

When Shopify Payments captures in EUR, it captures the rounded display price — €49.99. But the customer's bank card was issued by a bank that reports transactions in a slightly different way, or the card is a non-EUR card that does a secondary conversion. The €0.13 discrepancy often comes from the bank's own processing, not from Shopify. However, from the customer's perspective, they agreed to €49.99 and were charged a different amount.

Common sources of small discrepancies at charge time

Source Amount Discrepancy Who Controls It
Bank's secondary currency conversion (non-EUR card paying EUR) 1-3% Customer's bank — not Shopify or merchant
Shopify rounding difference vs exact conversion 0-0.5% Shopify's rounding rule setting
PayPal currency conversion fee 2.5-3.5% PayPal — not Shopify or merchant
International transaction fee (Visa/Mastercard) 1-3% Customer's card network and issuing bank

What merchants can do

  • Set fixed market prices to eliminate rounding entirely — the customer pays the exact amount shown
  • Use Shopify Payments to ensure capture happens in the displayed currency, eliminating the merchant-side conversion layer
  • Add a note at checkout for markets where currency conversion is unavoidable: "Final amount may vary slightly based on your bank's exchange rate"
  • For refunds: refund in the original captured amount, not the displayed amount — Shopify handles this correctly when processing refunds via Admin

Failure Mode 6: Local Payment Methods Disappear After Enabling Multi-Currency

iDEAL (Netherlands), Bancontact (Belgium), OXXO (Mexico), Boleto Bancário (Brazil), Konbini (Japan), and other local payment methods have strict country and currency requirements. Enabling Shopify Markets multi-currency without properly configuring each market's currency can cause these payment methods to disappear at checkout — even for customers in the correct country.

Why local payment methods hide at checkout

Local payment methods are surfaced at checkout based on a combination of: (1) the customer's detected country, (2) the cart's presentment currency, and (3) whether the payment method supports that currency in that country. If any of these don't align, the local payment method is hidden without an error message.

Local Payment Method Required Country Required Currency Common Failure Scenario
iDEAL Netherlands (NL) EUR Store primary currency is USD — Dutch customers see USD checkout, iDEAL doesn't appear
Bancontact Belgium (BE) EUR Same — primary currency USD, Belgian market not configured with EUR
OXXO Mexico (MX) MXN Merchant enabled Markets for Mexico but assigned USD not MXN — OXXO hidden
Boleto Bancário Brazil (BR) BRL Brazilian market configured but BRL not assigned — Boleto absent
Konbini Japan (JP) JPY Japan market with USD — Konbini hidden
Klarna DE, AT, NL, SE, etc. EUR, SEK (varies by country) Klarna only appears in Klarna-supported countries with correct currency; missing Markets currency assignment hides it

Fixing missing local payment methods

  1. Admin → Markets → select the market where the local payment method should appear (e.g., Netherlands)
  2. Under "Currency," set the correct local currency (EUR for Netherlands, MXN for Mexico)
  3. Admin → Settings → Payments → Shopify Payments → Manage → verify local payment methods are enabled for the relevant countries
  4. Test as a customer from that country: use an incognito window, switch country to Netherlands using the theme's country selector, and verify iDEAL appears at checkout
  5. If the payment method still doesn't appear, check that the customer's billing address country matches the payment method's required country — some local methods don't appear when billing country and IP country don't match
Local Payment Methods Require Shopify Payments — Not Available With Third-Party Gateways
iDEAL, Bancontact, OXXO, Boleto, Konbini, and Klarna on Shopify are provided through Shopify Payments. Merchants using a third-party gateway as their primary payment method cannot access these local payment methods through Shopify — they would need to integrate each method separately through the third-party gateway's own API, which is outside Shopify's standard configuration. If you need local payment methods, Shopify Payments must be your primary gateway in the relevant market.

Failure Mode 7: Headless Storefront — Prices Always Return in Store Primary Currency

Headless Shopify storefronts (Hydrogen, custom Next.js, or any build using the Storefront API) must explicitly pass country and currency context on every API request. Without the @inContext directive on Storefront API GraphQL queries, all prices return in the store's primary currency regardless of the customer's location — even when Shopify Markets is enabled with multiple currencies.

The @inContext directive — the most commonly missed requirement

The @inContext directive tells the Storefront API to return prices in the specified market's currency. Without it, the API behaves as if no market context exists and returns the base currency. This is the #1 headless multi-currency implementation mistake.

// WRONG — returns prices in store primary currency regardless of customer location
query ProductPrice {
  product(id: "gid://shopify/Product/123") {
    variants(first: 1) {
      nodes {
        price {
          amount
          currencyCode   # Always returns "USD" or store primary currency
        }
      }
    }
  }
}

// CORRECT — returns prices in the customer's market currency
query ProductPrice @inContext(country: DE, language: DE) {
  product(id: "gid://shopify/Product/123") {
    variants(first: 1) {
      nodes {
        price {
          amount
          currencyCode   # Returns "EUR" for German market
        }
        compareAtPrice {
          amount
          currencyCode
        }
      }
    }
  }
}

Passing dynamic country context based on customer location

// In Hydrogen (React) — pass country from localization context
import { useLocalization } from '@shopify/hydrogen';

function ProductPage() {
  const { country } = useLocalization();

  // The Hydrogen framework automatically applies @inContext
  // when you use the country from useLocalization()

  // For custom Storefront API calls, pass as a header:
  // 'Shopify-Storefront-Buyer-IP': customerIp (for geo-detection)
  // OR pass country explicitly in the @inContext directive
}

// Direct Storefront API call with country header (alternative to @inContext)
const response = await fetch(
  `https://${shop}.myshopify.com/api/2024-10/graphql.json`,
  {
    method: 'POST',
    headers: {
      'X-Shopify-Storefront-Access-Token': token,
      'Content-Type': 'application/json',
      // Shopify uses this to determine the buyer's country for pricing
      'Shopify-Storefront-Buyer-IP': customerIpAddress,
    },
    body: JSON.stringify({ query: PRODUCT_QUERY }),
  }
);

Cart creation with correct presentment currency

// WRONG — cart created without country context; checkout will use primary currency
mutation CreateCart($input: CartInput!) {
  cartCreate(input: $input) {
    cart { id checkoutUrl }
  }
}

// CORRECT — cart created with buyer identity country for currency context
mutation CreateCart($input: CartInput!, $country: CountryCode!) @inContext(country: $country) {
  cartCreate(input: $input) {
    cart {
      id
      checkoutUrl
      cost {
        totalAmount {
          amount
          currencyCode   # Confirms cart is in correct currency
        }
      }
    }
  }
}

// When country changes (e.g., user switches country selector):
mutation UpdateBuyerIdentity($cartId: ID!, $buyerIdentity: CartBuyerIdentityInput!) {
  cartBuyerIdentityUpdate(cartId: $cartId, buyerIdentity: $buyerIdentity) {
    cart {
      id
      cost {
        totalAmount { amount currencyCode }
      }
    }
  }
}
Storefront API inContext Requirements for Multi-Currency
The @inContext(country: X) directive must be applied to: (1) all product price queries, (2) all cart creation mutations, (3) all cart query operations that display prices. Missing it on any one of these causes a currency mismatch between what the customer sees on the product page and what the cart/checkout shows. Hydrogen handles this automatically when you use the localization context — but custom integrations must wire it manually on every query.

Failure Mode 8: Currency Not Updating After Country Selector Change

A customer uses the country/language selector to switch from United States (USD) to France (EUR). Product prices update visually to show EUR. But when they add the item to cart and view the cart, the price is still shown in USD. Or: the cart shows EUR but when they switch back to US, the cart doesn't revert — it stays in EUR for the rest of the session.

Why cart currency doesn't always follow the country selector

The country selector on a Shopify storefront does two things: (1) updates the display context for new page loads (currency formatting, language, regional content), and (2) should update the existing cart's presentment currency via the localization API. If the second step fails — due to a JavaScript error, a timing issue, or a custom implementation that doesn't call the right API — the cart retains its old currency even after the country switch.

Symptom Likely Cause Fix
Country switches but cart still shows old currency Custom country selector not calling /localization POST or Storefront API cartBuyerIdentityUpdate Ensure country form submits to /localization with country_code parameter, or call cartBuyerIdentityUpdate with countryCode
Currency updates on product pages but not in cart drawer Cart drawer fetches /cart.json which returns the cart's assigned currency — may differ from the display currency if the cart update was not completed Refresh cart data after the country switch to ensure the cart's presentment currency is current
Currency change causes cart to empty or reset Some Shopify themes create a new cart when the currency changes — this is by design when the new currency requires a market context switch This is expected Shopify behavior — inform customers in the UI that switching countries may reset the cart; offer to re-add items
Headless: country updates visually but Storefront API still returns old currency @inContext country not updated in subsequent queries after country switch Re-run queries with the new country code in @inContext; call cartBuyerIdentityUpdate with new countryCode

Testing that the country switch updates the cart correctly

  1. Add a product to cart while on US/USD
  2. Use the country selector to switch to Germany/EUR
  3. Open /cart.json in a new browser tab (for non-headless Shopify stores)
  4. Check the "currency" field in the JSON response — it should now show "EUR" not "USD"
  5. If it still shows "USD," the country selector is not updating the cart's presentment currency
  6. Check the Network tab during the country switch for a POST request to /localization with country_code=DE
  7. If no localization request fires, the country selector is display-only and not updating the cart context

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I show a currency conversion tool at checkout to explain the exchange rate?

On non-Plus plans, there is no native way to inject custom content into the checkout payment page. You can add messaging to the cart page (before checkout) explaining that the final charge amount may vary slightly from the displayed price due to your bank's exchange rate. On Plus plans, a Checkout UI Extension can add a custom text block near the payment methods section displaying the current exchange rate or a note about currency conversion. For most merchants, the better solution is to use Shopify Payments multi-currency capture, which eliminates the bank-side conversion entirely — customers are charged exactly what Shopify displays, with no additional FX layer.

Why does Shopify show "You will be charged in USD" even though I enabled EUR for my European market?

The "You will be charged in [currency]" notice in checkout reflects the actual capture currency, not the display currency. If this notice shows your primary currency (USD) instead of EUR, it means either (1) your payment gateway doesn't support multi-currency capture — only Shopify Payments does this natively, or (2) the cart's presentment currency was not properly set to EUR before checkout — the country selector didn't update the cart context. Check Admin → Markets → European Union → Currency to confirm EUR is assigned, then test by adding a product to cart with Germany selected as country and verifying the cart's currency is EUR before proceeding to checkout.

A customer was charged twice in different currencies — what happened?

This typically happens in one of two scenarios: (1) the customer initiated two separate checkout sessions (common when switching devices or browsers) — both sessions created separate authorization holds; (2) the customer completed checkout but then PayPal Express also fired a duplicate authorization from a previous attempt. Check Admin → Orders → filter by the customer's email — if two orders appear, one may have been created in error. If only one order exists but the customer sees two bank entries, one is likely an authorization hold from an abandoned checkout that will release in 3-7 business days. See the Payment Declined But Money Taken guide for the full explanation of authorization holds.

Does Shopify automatically detect a customer's country and switch currency?

Shopify Markets does attempt to detect the customer's country based on their IP address and redirect them to the appropriate market (with the correct currency). However, IP-based geolocation is imperfect — VPN users, corporate networks, and proxy servers often trigger incorrect country detection. Shopify shows a "Shop in [detected country]?" banner that the customer can dismiss. This detection updates the display currency but only updates the cart currency if the customer confirms the country switch via the selector. Customers on VPNs or who are traveling may need to manually select their country to see local pricing and payment methods.
