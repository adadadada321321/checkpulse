Pre-Order Checkout

Shopify Pre-Order Checkout Not Working — Partial Payment, Pre-Order App Errors, and Inventory Release Failures

Pre-orders are not a Shopify feature. They are a third-party app layer built on Checkout Extensions and inventory overrides. When a pre-order app fails, your Shopify checkout health shows 100% green — the payment goes through, the order is created, and the customer has no idea they just ordered something that won't ship for three months.

The structural blind spot: Shopify has no native pre-order concept. A "pre-order" is either (a) a product set to "continue selling when out of stock" with a third-party app relabeling the button and tracking expected fulfillment dates, or (b) a Checkout Extension that injects pre-order terms and deposit payment logic into the standard checkout. When the app layer breaks, checkout still works — the pre-order terms disappear, the customer pays full price expecting immediate delivery, and you have an order you can't fulfill on the timeline the customer assumed. There is no error. There is no abandoned cart. There is a future support ticket and a refund request.
📦
Pre-Order App Layer
Pre-order apps (PreOrder Now, Appikon, Timesact, Pre-Order Manager) work through two mechanisms: Checkout Extensions that inject terms and deposit toggles into checkout, and storefront scripts that relabel the Add to Cart button. Both break when themes change, when apps update, or when Checkout Extension permissions are revoked during a Shopify review.
💳
Partial Payment Layer
Shopify has no native deposit-at-checkout feature. Pre-order deposits are implemented as either a discounted variant (customer buys a "$50 deposit" SKU) or a Checkout Extension that modifies the order total before payment. Both methods have edge cases: variant deposits break when new product variants are added; extension deposits fail when the gateway rejects a modified order total.
📬
Pre-Order Communication Layer
Pre-order customers need different communication than standard orders: an order confirmation that explains the pre-order status and expected ship date, a shipping date update if timelines change, and a fulfillment notification when the item ships. When a pre-order app fails, these notifications either go out with wrong content (Shopify's default "ships in 3-5 days") or don't go out at all — the customer has no idea what's happening with their order.
Failure Analysis

6 Ways Shopify Pre-Order Checkout Fails Silently

Each failure mode allows the checkout to complete successfully while creating a customer experience problem that generates support tickets, refund requests, and chargebacks — often weeks after the order was placed.

Failure 01 Pre-Order Button Disappeared — Product Shows "Sold Out" Instead

A merchant updated their Shopify theme to the latest version. The theme update overwrote the pre-order app's Liquid template injection, removing the script that replaces "Sold Out" with "Pre-Order." Customers now see a greyed-out "Sold Out" button on every pre-order product. No add-to-cart, no checkout — the pre-order is completely invisible to customers.

Invisible because: Shopify's checkout monitoring, Google Search Console, and your analytics platform all show normal traffic to the product page. The only visible signal is a drop in pre-order conversions — which looks like reduced demand rather than a broken button. The merchant typically discovers it when a customer emails asking why the product is sold out when the website shows "coming soon."

Why it happens: Pre-order apps that use Liquid theme injection (replacing {% raw %}{{ 'sold_out' | t }}{% endraw %} in product templates) require a reinstall of the app's theme code after every theme update that touches the product template. Apps that use Checkout Extensions instead of theme injection are more resilient — the extension lives outside the theme layer and is not overwritten by theme updates. If your pre-order app uses Liquid injection, check whether the app has a "reinstall" or "sync theme" option in its dashboard after any theme update.

Fix: In your pre-order app dashboard, look for a "Theme integration" or "App embeds" section. Verify the app is connected to your current live theme (Shopify allows multiple themes, and apps sometimes remain connected to an older theme after a new one is published). If the app uses App Embeds, go to Online Store → Themes → Customize → App Embeds and verify the pre-order app embed is toggled on. If the app uses Liquid injection, follow the app's reinstallation instructions for your current theme.

Failure 02 Checkout Extension Missing — Customer Sees No Pre-Order Terms

A customer adds a pre-order item to their cart and proceeds through checkout. They see the standard Shopify checkout with no indication that the item is a pre-order. They pay full price expecting delivery in 5-7 business days. The item will not ship for 8 weeks. They contact support 10 days later asking where their order is.

The pre-order app was using a Checkout Extension to inject a banner above the payment step: "This item is a pre-order. Expected ship date: August 15, 2026." The extension was disabled when the merchant's Shopify plan was briefly downgraded for a billing issue and then re-upgraded — Checkout Extensions are sometimes disabled during plan changes and not automatically re-enabled.

Invisible because: Checkout completes successfully. No error, no abandoned cart, no alert. The pre-order app dashboard still shows the product as "active pre-order." The Checkout Extension status in the app may not reflect that it was disabled at the platform level. The only signal is customer complaints about unmet delivery expectations — which arrive weeks later when the delivery window passes.

Fix: Go to your Shopify admin → Sales Channels → Online Store → Checkout → Checkout Extensions (or the equivalent in your Shopify version). Verify your pre-order app's extension is listed as active. Also check in your pre-order app's dashboard under "Checkout integration" or "Extension status." After any Shopify plan change, billing issue, or checkout settings update, explicitly verify that all Checkout Extensions are still active. Run a test pre-order as a customer immediately after any plan or billing change.

Failure 03 Partial Payment Deposit Not Applying — Full Price Charged at Checkout

A merchant offers 20% deposits on pre-orders: customers pay $60 now and $240 at shipment for a $300 product. After adding three new product variants to an existing pre-order product, the deposit no longer applies at checkout — all three new variants charge the full $300. The merchant doesn't notice because the pre-order app dashboard still shows the product as "active with 20% deposit." The deposit configuration was not automatically applied to the new variants.

Pre-order deposit configurations are variant-specific in most apps. When new variants are added to a product, they are not automatically included in the existing deposit rule — the merchant must manually add the new variants to the pre-order deposit configuration in the app dashboard.

Invisible because: Checkout completes and customers pay — just at the wrong price. Customers who expected to pay a deposit and pay the balance at shipment instead pay the full price upfront. Some notice and contact support. Many don't notice until they expect a refund at shipment time that never arrives. The app dashboard continues showing the product as having an active deposit.

Fix: After adding any new variant to a pre-order product with deposit pricing, immediately check the pre-order app dashboard and explicitly add the new variants to the deposit rule. Also verify by placing a test order on each new variant as a customer. Additionally, configure the app to send you an alert when new variants are added to pre-order products — some apps have this as a notification option. If your app uses a discount-variant approach (a "$60 deposit" variant instead of a Checkout Extension), ensure the deposit variant is created for every size/color combination.

Failure 04 Mixed Cart Blocks Checkout — In-Stock and Pre-Order Items Together

A customer adds a pre-order item (expected to ship in 10 weeks) and two in-stock items to their cart. When they try to check out, they see an error: "Your cart contains items with different shipping timelines. Please check out in-stock items separately." The customer is confused — they see no pre-order label on the product page, only a slightly different button color. They abandon the cart.

Some pre-order apps block mixed-cart checkout by design to prevent the customer confusion of receiving a partial shipment. This is configurable — the block is a setting that can be enabled or disabled. When enabled without adequate customer communication, it causes checkout abandonment from customers who didn't know they were creating a "mixed cart."

Invisible because: The checkout block is intentional behavior, but the customer experience is indistinguishable from a checkout error. Your analytics show checkout abandonment at the cart or checkout step with no error code. Cart abandonment emails go out offering a discount — which doesn't address the actual problem (the customer doesn't know they need to separate their cart).

Fix: If you use mixed-cart blocking, add a visible warning on the product page and in the cart for pre-order items: "This is a pre-order item. To add it to your cart, you'll need to checkout separately from any in-stock items." Add the warning in the cart drawer as well — not just on the product page. Alternatively, disable mixed-cart blocking and configure your pre-order app to automatically send a split-shipment notice to customers with mixed carts, explaining that their order will arrive in two shipments. Many merchants find that transparent communication about split shipments is preferable to blocking checkout.

Failure 05 Pre-Order Inventory Counter Goes Negative or Doesn't Decrement

A merchant sets up a limited pre-order: 100 units available for pre-order. The pre-order app is supposed to track pre-orders against the 100-unit cap and close pre-orders when the limit is reached. After 137 pre-orders, the app still shows the product as available for pre-order. The inventory counter in the app dashboard shows -37 units. The merchant discovers this when their supplier can only deliver 100 units and they have 137 orders to fulfill.

Pre-order inventory tracking is handled by the app, not by Shopify's native inventory system. When two customers check out simultaneously with the last available pre-order unit, both orders can succeed if the app's inventory decrement has a race condition — the app checks available units, both see "1 unit available," both proceed, and the unit count goes to -1.

Invisible because: Shopify's inventory system is typically set to "continue selling when out of stock" for pre-order products (this is required for the pre-order to work at all). The pre-order cap is enforced entirely by the app layer. The Shopify admin shows no inventory problem — it just shows an increasing number of pre-orders being fulfilled by a supplier that doesn't have enough units.

Fix: For limited pre-orders, verify your app uses atomic inventory decrements (a database transaction that locks the inventory count during each pre-order). Check the app's documentation or contact their support to confirm how they handle concurrent pre-orders. Additionally, set up a safety net: use Shopify's native inventory tracking for a secondary cap — set inventory to your pre-order limit (e.g., 100) and enable the "stop selling when out of stock" setting as a hard floor. The app will still allow pre-orders above zero, and Shopify's native system will stop them at zero. This dual-layer cap prevents overselling even if the app has a race condition.

Failure 06 Pre-Order Confirmation Email Says "Ships in 3-5 Days"

A customer places a pre-order for a product shipping in 12 weeks. They receive Shopify's standard order confirmation email: "Thank you for your order! Your items will be shipped within 3-5 business days." Twelve days later, the customer has not received their order. They contact support. The merchant has to explain they ordered a pre-order item shipping in 3 months. The customer is furious — "nothing on your website or email said anything about a pre-order."

Shopify's native order notification emails do not know about pre-order status. They send the same templated confirmation to all orders. Pre-order apps that work through Checkout Extensions can intercept and modify the order confirmation — but this requires the app to have "email customization" or "notification override" capability, and it must be explicitly enabled.

Invisible because: The order confirmation email is sent automatically by Shopify. The merchant never reviews individual confirmation emails. The pre-order app dashboard shows the order in the "pre-order" queue. The customer's email inbox shows a standard order confirmation with a misleading shipping timeline. The disconnect between what the app knows and what the customer's email says is entirely invisible until the customer contacts support 10-14 days later.

Fix: There are two approaches. First, customize Shopify's standard order confirmation email template (Online Store → Notifications → Order Confirmation) to include a conditional block: if the order contains a tagged pre-order product (apps typically add a "pre-order" tag to pre-order orders), show a custom message: "One or more items in your order are pre-order items. See the expected ship date below." Second, configure your pre-order app to send a separate pre-order confirmation email immediately after the standard Shopify notification. Most pre-order apps have this as an option — it sends a second email with the estimated ship date, deposit summary (if applicable), and what to expect. Some merchants disable Shopify's native confirmation email for pre-order orders and rely entirely on the app's custom confirmation — check your app's documentation for this option.

Pre-Order Audit

8-Step Pre-Order Checkout Readiness Checklist

Run this audit when you set up a new pre-order product, change your pre-order app, update your Shopify theme, or notice an unexpected drop in pre-order conversions.

01
Place a test pre-order as a logged-out customer in an incognito window. This is the only reliable way to verify the entire pre-order flow. Verify: the Add to Cart button says "Pre-Order" (or your configured label), the cart shows a pre-order badge or notice, checkout shows pre-order terms and expected ship date, the correct amount is charged (deposit or full price), and the order confirmation email correctly identifies the item as a pre-order. Do this test after every theme update, app update, and Shopify plan change.
02
Verify Checkout Extension status after every plan or billing change. Checkout Extensions are sometimes disabled during Shopify plan changes, downgrade/upgrade cycles, and billing issue resolutions. After any account-level change in Shopify, check Online Store → Checkout → App Extensions and confirm your pre-order app's extension is listed as active. Also check the extension status in your pre-order app's dashboard — some apps surface this as a "Checkout integration active" indicator.
03
Check theme integration after every theme update or new theme publish. After publishing a theme update, open your pre-order app dashboard and look for a "theme integration" or "theme sync" status indicator. If the app uses App Embeds, go to Themes → Customize → App Embeds and confirm the pre-order embed is toggled on for the new theme version. If the app uses Liquid template injection, re-run the app's theme installation process. Apps that use Checkout Extensions instead of theme injection are immune to theme changes — consider migrating if your current app requires frequent theme reinstallation.
04
Add new product variants to deposit rules manually. After adding any new variant (size, color, bundle) to a product with deposit pricing, immediately go to your pre-order app dashboard and add the new variant to the deposit configuration. Place a test order on the new variant to verify the deposit amount applies correctly. Consider setting a calendar reminder for this check whenever product variants are modified — it is the most common cause of "deposit not applying" support tickets.
05
Set a hard inventory cap for limited pre-orders. For pre-orders with a unit limit, use a two-layer cap: your pre-order app's internal counter as the primary cap, and Shopify's native inventory tracking as the fallback. Set Shopify's inventory to your pre-order unit limit and change "when out of stock" to "stop selling." The pre-order app will use its own counter first, and Shopify's native limit will act as a safety net for race conditions. Monitor the app's counter and Shopify's inventory level weekly to ensure they stay in sync.
06
Customize the order confirmation email for pre-order orders. Add a conditional block to Shopify's Order Confirmation notification template (Online Store → Notifications → Order Confirmation) that shows pre-order information when the order is tagged with your pre-order app's tag. Alternatively, configure your pre-order app to send a separate pre-order confirmation email — most apps offer this option. The goal: every customer who places a pre-order must receive a confirmation that explicitly says it is a pre-order and gives the estimated ship date.
07
Add visible pre-order labels to the product page, cart, and checkout. Pre-order customers need to see the pre-order status at three points: on the product page (button label + expected ship date below the button), in the cart (a badge or notice on the line item), and in checkout (a Checkout Extension block above the payment step). If any of these three touchpoints is missing, a fraction of customers will complete checkout without understanding they ordered a pre-order item. Configure your app to show all three — not just the button label.
08
Monitor pre-order checkout health separately from standard checkout health. Pre-order checkout failures are app-layer failures — they don't show up in standard checkout health metrics. Create a weekly monitoring routine: check your pre-order app dashboard for active pre-orders, open rates on pre-order confirmation emails, and any "pre-order extension error" notifications. Use CheckPulse to monitor your standard checkout continuously (catching payment failures, button failures, and session issues on the standard checkout flow), and combine it with weekly manual pre-order checkout testing to cover both layers.

Cluster Context

How Pre-Order Checkout Connects to Other Checkout Problem Clusters

Pre-order checkout sits at the intersection of several checkout problem categories. Understanding the connections helps you audit the right layer for each failure.

Pre-Orders → Inventory Errors

Pre-order inventory management relies on "continue selling when out of stock" being enabled — the same setting that causes standard inventory errors when accidentally left on for non-pre-order products. If a standard product is accidentally set to continue selling when out of stock, it may appear to support pre-orders when it doesn't. Conversely, if a pre-order product has this setting accidentally disabled, pre-orders stop working immediately. See the Inventory Error guide for how to audit inventory settings across your catalog and distinguish intentional pre-order oversell settings from accidental oversell misconfigurations.

Pre-Orders → Checkout Extensions

Pre-order Checkout Extensions are a specific use case of Shopify's broader Checkout Extensions framework. Understanding how Checkout Extensions work — what they can and cannot do, how they interact with themes, and how their status is managed in Shopify admin — is essential for diagnosing pre-order extension failures. See the Checkout Extensions guide for the complete picture of Checkout Extension failures, permission issues, and theme-extension interactions.

Pre-Orders → Subscription Checkout

Some merchants offer "pre-order subscriptions" — a customer pre-orders an item and signs up for recurring delivery at the same time. This combines the complexity of pre-order app logic with subscription app logic on a single checkout session. If both apps use Checkout Extensions, they can conflict with each other. See the Subscription Checkout guide for how to identify and resolve multi-app conflicts in the Checkout Extensions layer.

Pre-Orders → Order Confirmation

The most common pre-order customer complaint — "I didn't know it was a pre-order" — is fundamentally an order confirmation problem. The standard Shopify order confirmation email doesn't surface pre-order status. See the Order Confirmation guide for how to customize Shopify's notification templates and how to configure post-order communication flows for non-standard order types.

FAQ

Pre-Order Checkout — Common Questions

Why is my Shopify pre-order checkout not working?

Pre-orders are not native to Shopify — they require a third-party app. The most common reasons pre-order checkout breaks: a theme update overwrote the app's button injection, a Checkout Extension was disabled during a plan or billing change, a new product variant was added without being included in the pre-order configuration, or the app itself experienced an outage.

Diagnose by placing a test order as a logged-out customer in incognito mode. Verify the button label, the checkout pre-order terms block, the charge amount, and the confirmation email. If any of these are missing or wrong, use the failure analysis above to identify which layer broke.

Why is my Shopify pre-order partial payment (deposit) not working?

Deposit pricing is not a Shopify feature — it's implemented by your pre-order app using either a "deposit variant" (a separate SKU at the deposit price) or a Checkout Extension that modifies the order total. Both break when new variants are added without updating the deposit configuration, when the Checkout Extension is disabled, or when the payment gateway rejects a modified order total.

After adding any new product variant, immediately check your pre-order app dashboard to confirm the deposit rule applies to the new variant. Place a test order on the new variant before opening it to customers. If deposits are working on some variants but not others, the deposit rule almost always needs to be manually extended to the missing variants.

Why are customers not seeing the pre-order label on my Shopify product?

The pre-order button label is injected by your app through either Liquid theme code or a JavaScript snippet. Both methods break after theme updates. Check your pre-order app dashboard for a "theme integration status" or "app embeds" indicator. If you see a warning or error there, follow the app's theme reinstallation steps for your current theme.

Also check Shopify admin → Online Store → Themes → Customize → App Embeds. If the pre-order app's embed is toggled off, turn it back on and save. This is the most common fix after a theme update.

How do I fix a mixed cart problem with pre-order and in-stock items on Shopify?

Mixed cart blocking is a setting in your pre-order app — it is intentional, not a bug. If you want to allow mixed carts, disable the mixed cart block in the app settings and add a notice in the cart that explains split shipments ("Your order will ship in two parts: in-stock items within 5 days, pre-order items on [date]"). If you want to keep the block, add a visible warning on pre-order product pages and in the cart: "This is a pre-order item — it must be checked out separately from in-stock items."

How do I check if my Shopify pre-order checkout is working correctly?

The only reliable check is a live test order: open your store in an incognito window as a logged-out customer, navigate to a pre-order product, and complete a purchase using a test payment method. Verify: button label says "Pre-Order," checkout shows pre-order terms and estimated ship date, the charge amount is correct (deposit or full price), and the confirmation email explicitly mentions the pre-order status and expected ship date. If any of these four checks fail, use the Failure Analysis section above to identify the broken layer.

Run this test after every theme update, app update, and Shopify plan or billing change. Pre-order checkout is fragile — it is a multi-app layer on top of the standard checkout, and any change to the underlying layers can silently break it.
