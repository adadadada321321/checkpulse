Subscription Renewals

Shopify Subscription Renewal Failed — Failed Rebill, Dunning Not Working, and Card Update Flow Errors

Shopify subscription signup goes through checkout. Subscription renewal does not. When a renewal fails, there is no checkout error page, no abandoned cart, no visible signal in your storefront — there is a failed order in admin and a subscriber quietly cancelling because they never received a dunning email that worked.

The structural blind spot: Every checkout monitoring tool — including CheckPulse — monitors the checkout flow. Subscription renewals bypass checkout entirely. They are direct API calls from your subscription app (ReCharge, Bold, Appstle, Shopify Subscriptions) to the payment gateway, using a stored credential. A renewal failure leaves no trace in checkout analytics, no abandoned cart metric, and no visible error to any customer. The only signal is a failed order in your subscription app's admin — which you only see if you are actively looking for it.
🔁
Renewal Billing Layer
Subscription renewals are direct gateway charges against a stored payment credential. They occur outside Shopify checkout. Failure modes are different from purchase failures: card expiry, soft declines from banks flagging recurring charges, credential vaults out of sync between Shopify and the subscription app, and gateway-level recurring billing blocks.
📬
Dunning Management Layer
Dunning is the automated sequence of emails and retries that attempts to recover a failed renewal. It fails when emails land in spam, when retry schedules are too short, when the from-address is unrecognized, or when the dunning sequence fires only once. A single dunning email recovers roughly 10–15% of failed renewals. A five-email sequence over 14 days recovers 40–60%.
💳
Card Update Flow Layer
The card update link in a dunning email points to the subscription app's payment portal — not the Shopify customer account page. A customer who updates their card in their Shopify account has not updated their subscription billing credential. Card update links expire in 24–72 hours by default. Both of these facts are invisible to customers and generate large volumes of confused support tickets.
Failure Analysis

6 Ways Shopify Subscription Renewals Fail Silently

Each failure mode produces a churned subscriber and a confused support ticket. The common thread: the failure happens entirely outside the customer-facing storefront, making it invisible until the subscriber contacts you or simply disappears.

Failure 01 Card Expired at Renewal — Subscriber Silently Cancelled

A subscriber signed up 14 months ago with a card that expires this month. On the renewal date, the subscription app attempts the charge. The gateway returns a hard decline: card expired. The dunning sequence fires — but the email goes to an inbox the subscriber checks infrequently. Three days later, the dunning sequence exhausts its retries and the subscription is cancelled. The subscriber notices two weeks later when their product runs out. They contact support. The merchant has no way to recover the subscription automatically — the subscriber must re-subscribe from scratch.

Invisible because: The failure happens server-side. No checkout page was involved. The subscriber's storefront experience was unaffected — the store loaded fine, products showed correctly, and no error appeared anywhere. The only visible signal was a "Payment failed" order status in the subscription app's admin panel, which shows dozens of orders per day.

Fix: Enable pre-dunning card expiry warnings. Most subscription apps can flag renewals where the stored card expires before the next billing date and send a proactive "your card expires soon — please update before your next renewal" email 30 days in advance. Proactive warnings before a renewal fails convert at 3–5x the rate of post-failure dunning emails because the subscriber is not yet in a failed state and the email carries no negative connotation. Check your subscription app settings for "card expiry notifications" or "proactive dunning."

Failure 02 Bank Soft Decline — Dunning Retries Never Succeed Because Schedule Is Wrong

A subscriber's renewal fails with a soft decline: "do not honor" — a generic bank block that is usually temporary. The subscription app retries the charge 24 hours later. The bank blocks it again. The retry fires again the next day. Three retries in three consecutive days all fail because the bank's block lasts 5–7 days. The subscription app exhausts its retry limit, cancels the subscription, and sends the final dunning email informing the subscriber their subscription has ended.

Invisible because: Soft declines are indistinguishable from hard declines in the subscription app's order list unless you look at the decline code for each failed renewal. "Do not honor" is a soft decline that resolves itself — retrying after 5 days instead of 24 hours would have succeeded. The default retry schedule in most subscription apps (daily for 3–7 days) is too short and too frequent for this class of soft decline.

Fix: Configure your subscription app's retry schedule to space retries further apart: day 1, day 3, day 7, day 14 is more effective than day 1, day 2, day 3 for soft declines. Review the decline codes on your failed renewals — most subscription apps expose this in their admin. "Do not honor" (05), "insufficient funds" (51), and "exceeds withdrawal limit" (61) are all soft declines; "card expired" (54), "card lost" (41), and "card stolen" (43) are hard declines. Hard declines should trigger an immediate card update request; soft declines should trigger a spaced retry schedule with no card update request until day 7.

Failure 03 Dunning Emails Land in Spam or Go to the Wrong Address

A renewal fails. The subscription app sends the first dunning email. The subscriber never sees it because: (a) it landed in their spam folder — dunning emails have high spam scores due to payment language and CTA links, especially when sent from a domain the subscriber doesn't recognize; or (b) the subscriber changed their email address since signing up, and the dunning email goes to an old inbox the subscriber no longer checks. The dunning sequence completes without any subscriber engagement. The subscription cancels.

Fix for spam: Send dunning emails from a from-address the subscriber recognizes — your store domain, not your subscription app's generic domain. In ReCharge, configure the "from" email address in Settings → Notifications → Sender settings to use your store domain (requires email authentication setup: DKIM and SPF records). The subject line "Action required: update your payment method" performs worse than "Your [store name] subscription — payment update needed" because the former pattern-matches common spam subject lines. Include the subscriber's first name and the product they subscribe to in the subject line — personal, specific subjects have significantly higher open rates than generic payment notices.

Fix for wrong address: After a renewal failure, if the first dunning email has a 0% open rate after 48 hours, send the dunning sequence to any secondary contact method you have — an SMS number, or the billing address email if it differs from the account email. For high-value subscribers, a manual outreach from your support email (not the automated dunning sequence) often recovers the relationship when automated sequences fail.

Failure 04 Card Update Link Expired Before Customer Used It

A subscriber receives a dunning email with a "Update payment method" link. They intend to click it but get distracted. They return 36 hours later and click the link — it shows an error: "This link has expired." They do not know how to update their subscription payment method through any other path. They contact support. Support sends a fresh link manually from the subscription app admin. The subscriber updates the card. Two subscription renewals were already missed and the subscriber lost access for 4 days.

Default card update link expiry is 24–72 hours in most subscription apps. This is far too short — most subscribers who see a dunning email but do not act immediately intend to act "later today" which often becomes "two days later."

Fix: Extend card update link expiry to a minimum of 7 days in your subscription app settings. In ReCharge: Settings → Customer Portal → Link expiration. In Shopify Subscriptions: the customer portal link does not expire as long as the subscription account is active — verify with your app. In Bold and Appstle: check the notification settings for card update link lifetime. Additionally, the dunning email should explain: "If this link has expired, log in to your account at [store.com/account] and go to Subscriptions → Payment Method to update your card." A fallback path instruction reduces "link expired" support tickets significantly.

Failure 05 Customer Updates Card in Shopify Account — Renewal Still Fails

A subscriber receives a dunning email and is told to update their payment method. They navigate to their account on your store, go to "Payment methods," and update their card. They assume the problem is fixed. The next renewal attempt still fails with the same card-expired decline. They contact support furious: "I already updated my card — why is it still failing?"

The cause: Shopify's native customer account payment methods and the subscription app's billing credential vault are separate systems. Updating a card in the Shopify customer account does not update the card stored in ReCharge, Bold, Appstle, or Shopify Subscriptions. The subscriber must update their card specifically through the subscription app's customer portal — a different URL and a different interface.

Invisible because: The subscriber did exactly what they were asked to do — updated their payment method. From their perspective, the problem is fixed. The distinction between "Shopify account payment method" and "subscription billing credential" is a technical implementation detail that should never be the subscriber's problem to understand.

Fix: The dunning email must link directly to the subscription app's card update portal, not to the Shopify customer account. If your dunning email says "update your payment method at [store.com/account]," that is wrong and will generate this exact support ticket. The link must go to the subscription customer portal (e.g., store.com/tools/recurring/login for ReCharge, or the Shopify Subscriptions customer portal URL). Add explicit text: "Use this specific link to update your subscription payment — updating your card in your regular account will not update your subscription."

Failure 06 Trial-to-Paid Conversion Fails — Free Trial Ends Without Billing

A subscriber signed up for a 14-day free trial. At signup, they provided a payment method — but the card was not charged. On day 15, the subscription app attempts the first billing charge. The card declines (it was a prepaid Visa with $0 balance — the subscriber intended to update their card before the trial ended but forgot). The trial period is now over and the subscription enters a dunning cycle immediately. The subscriber contacts support to say their trial "disappeared without warning."

Invisible because: The trial itself worked perfectly. The store delivered trial access with no issues. The failure occurs at the first billing event, which happens invisibly in the background. There is no "trial is ending" checkout page or confirmation — the transition from trial to paid is a server-side billing event, just like any renewal.

Fix: Send a dedicated "your free trial ends in 3 days — your card will be charged on [date]" email to trial subscribers at the 3-day and 1-day marks before billing. This email should include the exact amount, the card being charged (last 4 digits), and a link to update the payment method if needed. Some merchants also send a "your trial ends today" email on the morning of the charge. This dramatically reduces failed trial-to-paid conversions because it gives the subscriber an opportunity to update a bad card before the first charge attempt — rather than responding to a failed charge dunning email afterwards.

Renewal Audit

8-Step Subscription Renewal Readiness Checklist

Run this audit when you set up a subscription product, change your subscription app, or notice a spike in subscriber churn or "payment failed" orders.

01
Enable pre-dunning card expiry notifications. In your subscription app settings, find "card expiry notifications" or "proactive dunning." Configure it to email subscribers 30 days before their stored card expires. This single setting eliminates the majority of card-expired hard declines at renewal — a renewal that was going to fail in 30 days becomes a proactive card update instead.
02
Configure a smart retry schedule for soft declines. Set your retry schedule to space retries across the billing cycle: attempt on day 1, day 3, day 7, and day 14 after the failed renewal — not daily for 3 consecutive days. Review your failed renewal decline codes monthly and adjust retry timing for the most common soft decline types in your subscriber base.
03
Set dunning email from-address to your store domain. In your subscription app notifications settings, configure the "from" email address to use a domain the subscriber recognizes (e.g., hello@yourstore.com). This requires adding DKIM and SPF records for the sending domain. The deliverability improvement for dunning emails when sent from a trusted domain is significant — test by checking spam placement rates on your current dunning emails.
04
Extend card update link expiry to 7 days minimum. Find the card update link expiry setting in your subscription app (often under Customer Portal or Notification settings). Change it from the default 24–72 hours to at least 7 days. Add a fallback instruction in all dunning emails: "If this link has expired, log in to [subscription portal URL] to update your payment method."
05
Verify dunning email links go to the subscription portal, not Shopify account. Open your dunning email template in your subscription app. Click every link. Confirm the "update payment method" CTA goes to the subscription app's customer portal (e.g., store.com/tools/recurring/login for ReCharge) — not to /account. Add explicit text explaining the distinction so subscribers who try to update in the wrong place are redirected before they give up.
06
Configure a 5-step dunning sequence, not a 1-step sequence. Review your dunning email sequence length. If your app is set to send only one email after a failed renewal, you are recovering only a fraction of recoverable churned subscribers. Configure at least: day 1 (soft notice), day 3 (update urgency), day 7 (direct card update request), day 10 (subscription at risk), day 13 (final notice before cancellation). Each email should increase in urgency and specificity.
07
Add trial-ending email notifications before the first charge. If you offer free trials, configure "trial ending soon" emails at 3 days and 1 day before the first billing charge. Include the exact charge amount, the card on file (last 4 digits), and a direct link to update the payment method. This eliminates the "my trial disappeared" support ticket category and significantly improves trial-to-paid conversion rates.
08
Monitor checkout availability separately from renewal monitoring. Renewal failures are subscription-layer failures — they require monitoring in your subscription app dashboard, not checkout monitoring. However, checkout monitoring remains critical for the initial subscription signup flow, the card update portal (which uses a checkout-like UI), and any repurchase flows after a subscription lapses. Keep both monitoring channels active — they catch different failure classes.

Cluster Context

How Subscription Renewals Connect to the Subscription Checkout Cluster

Subscription billing has two distinct failure surfaces: the initial checkout (one-time event) and the renewal cycle (recurring event). Each requires different monitoring and different recovery strategies.

Renewals → Initial Subscription Checkout

The initial subscription checkout is the only part of the subscription lifecycle that goes through Shopify's checkout flow. App conflicts, payment method restrictions, and checkout errors at this stage are covered by the Subscription Checkout guide. If initial signups are failing, checkout monitoring will catch it. If renewals are failing, you need to look in your subscription app's admin.

Renewals → Payment Methods

Renewal failures are often caused by payment method restrictions that were not present at the initial signup — card expiry, card replacement, bank blocks on recurring charges. Understanding which payment methods are most prone to renewal failures (prepaid cards, cards from certain banks) informs your dunning strategy and your upfront payment method requirements. See the Payment Methods guide for how different payment types behave at checkout and how to guide customers toward payment methods with better renewal success rates.

Renewals → Returns and Refunds

A subscriber who churns due to a failed renewal and then re-subscribes may request a refund for the period they lost access — even if the access lapse was caused by their own failed payment. Having a clear policy for "access lapse due to payment failure" reduces refund disputes. See the Returns and Refunds guide for how to communicate refund policies in ways that reduce customer friction after a payment failure.

Renewals → International Checkout

International subscribers face additional renewal failure risks: currency conversion differences between the original charge and the renewal amount (especially for annual plans where exchange rates shifted), bank blocks on foreign recurring charges that are stricter than domestic blocks, and dunning email deliverability issues in some international markets. See the International Checkout guide for the full picture of cross-border checkout and billing challenges.

FAQ

Subscription Renewal Failures — Common Questions

Why did my Shopify subscription renewal payment fail?

The most common causes: card expired between signup and renewal (especially on annual plans), the subscriber's bank flagged the recurring charge as suspicious and issued a temporary block, the subscriber's card reached its credit limit on the billing date, or the payment credential in the subscription app's vault is out of sync with the subscriber's actual current card.

Check the decline code in your subscription app's admin for each failed renewal. Hard declines (card expired, card closed, card reported stolen) require the subscriber to provide a new card — retrying will not work. Soft declines (do not honor, insufficient funds, exceeds limit) can be recovered with a spaced retry schedule.

Why are dunning emails not working for failed Shopify subscription renewals?

The most common dunning failures: emails landing in spam (sent from an unrecognized domain), emails going to an outdated address in the subscription app's record, the dunning sequence being too short (one or two emails), and the card update link in the email having expired before the subscriber clicked it.

Fixes: send dunning emails from your store domain (not your subscription app's generic domain), use a 5-email sequence spread over 14 days, extend card update link expiry to 7 days, and add the subscriber's first name and product name to the email subject line to increase open rates.

How do I update the payment method for a Shopify subscription?

The critical distinction: Shopify's native customer account (/account) and the subscription app's customer portal are separate systems with separate payment credential vaults. Updating a card in the Shopify customer account does NOT update the subscription billing credential — the subscriber must use the subscription portal link, which is different from the standard account page.

The subscription portal URL depends on your app: ReCharge users go to store.com/tools/recurring/login; Shopify Subscriptions users access it through the customer account subscriptions tab; Bold and Appstle have their own portal URLs. Always link directly to the subscription portal from dunning emails — never link to /account and assume subscribers will find the right payment method section.

What is the difference between a soft decline and hard decline in subscription renewal?

Hard declines mean the card cannot be charged under any circumstances — do not retry. Common hard declines: card expired (54), card reported lost (41), card reported stolen (43), card account closed. For hard declines, send a card update request immediately and do not retry — multiple failed charges on a closed card can trigger your gateway's fraud filters.

Soft declines mean the charge failed temporarily and may succeed on a retry. Common soft declines: do not honor (05), insufficient funds (51), exceeds withdrawal limit (61). For soft declines, retry on day 3 and day 7. Do not send a card update request on the first soft decline — a spaced retry often succeeds without requiring the subscriber to take any action.

How do I reduce Shopify subscription churn from failed renewals?

The highest-ROI interventions in order: (1) Pre-dunning card expiry warnings 30 days before a card on file expires — this is proactive and converts at 3–5x post-failure dunning. (2) Smart retry scheduling: space retries on day 1, day 3, day 7, day 14 rather than daily for 3 days. (3) 5-email dunning sequence with escalating urgency rather than 1–2 emails. (4) Card update link expiry extended to 7 days. (5) Visa Account Updater and Mastercard Automatic Billing Updater — services that push new card numbers automatically when a subscriber's card is replaced, without any subscriber action required. Check whether your payment gateway supports these services.

Involuntary churn from failed renewals is typically 1–3% of active subscribers per month for subscription merchants without proactive dunning. Merchants with mature dunning programs reduce this to 0.2–0.5%. The difference at scale is significant — on 1,000 active subscribers at $50/month, the gap is $400–1,300 in monthly recurring revenue.
