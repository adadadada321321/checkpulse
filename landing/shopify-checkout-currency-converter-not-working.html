Shopify Checkout Currency Converter Not Working — 8 Causes Explained

Customers seeing EUR prices but getting charged in USD? Shopify Markets currency not applying at checkout? Exchange rate showing the wrong amount? Here are all 8 root causes for Shopify multi-currency checkout failures — for merchants and developers.

Updated June 2026 8 failure modes Shopify Markets · Third-Party Converters · Exchange Rate · Shopify Payments · Headless Storefront API

Shopify's multi-currency system has one critical misunderstanding at its core: third-party currency converter apps cannot change what Shopify actually charges at checkout. Apps like Bold Currency Switcher, Auto Currency Switcher, and BEST Currency Converter change the displayed price on your storefront using JavaScript — but when a customer clicks "Check out," Shopify's checkout always uses the store's primary currency unless you have Shopify Payments enabled with Shopify Markets properly configured.

The result: customers see €89 on the product page and get charged $97 at checkout. Eight distinct failure modes all produce this same visible symptom but have completely different root causes and different fixes.

Two Completely Different Multi-Currency Systems
Display-only (third-party apps): JavaScript converts displayed storefront prices. Checkout always uses store's primary currency. Customer bank does the FX conversion. Works with any payment gateway.

True multi-currency (Shopify Markets + Shopify Payments): Shopify actually charges the customer in their local currency. Requires Shopify Payments as the active gateway. Exchange rate set and locked by Shopify. No FX conversion at the bank for supported currencies.

Check which system your store uses: If you have Shopify Payments active and Markets configured with local currencies — you have true multi-currency. If you have a third-party app installed and use Stripe/PayPal/Braintree — you have display-only.

Failure Mode 1: Third-Party Currency Converter Apps Are Display-Only — Checkout Always Reverts to Store Currency

The most common multi-currency complaint: "My store shows prices in EUR/GBP/AUD but customers get charged in USD at checkout." This is the expected behavior of every third-party currency converter app on the Shopify App Store.

Why third-party currency apps cannot change the checkout currency

Shopify's checkout is a sandboxed environment hosted on Shopify's infrastructure. Theme JavaScript does not execute inside the checkout. Third-party currency apps work by injecting a script into your storefront theme that replaces displayed price text with converted values. When the customer lands on the checkout page, they have left the storefront — the app's script no longer runs. Checkout reads the actual Shopify prices in the store's primary currency.

App What It Changes What Checkout Uses
Bold Currency Switcher Displayed price on storefront only Store primary currency
Auto Currency Switcher Displayed price on storefront only Store primary currency
BEST Currency Converter Displayed price on storefront only Store primary currency
Shopify Markets + Shopify Payments Actual checkout price + storefront display Local currency (true multi-currency)

What to do

  • If you need to actually charge customers in local currencies: migrate to Shopify Markets with Shopify Payments (see Failure Mode 2)
  • If display-only is acceptable: ensure the currency app shows a clear disclaimer at checkout — something like "Prices are for reference only. You will be charged in USD." Many merchants add this via the checkout's "Additional policies" field in Admin → Settings → Checkout
  • If you use Shopify Payments but are still seeing display-only behavior: your Markets configuration may be incomplete (see Failure Mode 3)
Consumer Protection Risk
In the EU, UK, and Australia, showing prices in a local currency and then charging in a different currency without clear disclosure can violate consumer protection regulations. The EU's Omnibus Directive and the UK's Consumer Rights Act both require that the final price shown to the customer matches what they are charged. If you operate a display-only currency converter without a disclosure, this creates legal risk in these markets.

Failure Mode 2: Shopify Markets Local Currency Requires Shopify Payments — Third-Party Gateway Kills It

The second most common scenario: a merchant enables Shopify Markets, configures local currencies for each market, and sees prices in EUR on the storefront — but checkout still charges in USD. The cause is almost always the payment gateway.

Shopify Payments is mandatory for local currency charging

Shopify Markets can display local currencies on the storefront without Shopify Payments. But the ability to charge in those local currencies — so that the customer's bank receives a EUR transaction, not a USD transaction — requires Shopify Payments to be the active payment method processing the transaction. Stripe, PayPal, Braintree, Authorize.net, and every other third-party gateway always settle in the store's primary currency.

Gateway Markets Storefront Display Actual Charge Currency
Shopify Payments (active) Local currency Local currency
Shopify Payments (pending/suspended) Local currency display only Falls back to primary currency
Stripe Local currency display only Store primary currency only
PayPal Local currency display only Store primary currency only
Braintree / Adyen / other Local currency display only Store primary currency only

Diagnosis steps

  1. Go to Admin → Settings → Payments
  2. Check if Shopify Payments shows status "Active" — not "Pending", "Paused", or "Suspended"
  3. If Shopify Payments is not available in your country, you cannot use Shopify Markets for true local currency charging — display-only via a third-party app is your only option
  4. If Shopify Payments is active but local currencies still aren't charging: continue to Failure Mode 3 to check Markets configuration
Countries Where Shopify Payments Is Available (2026)
Shopify Payments is available in: Australia, Austria, Belgium, Canada, Czech Republic, Denmark, Finland, France, Germany, Hong Kong SAR, Ireland, Italy, Japan, Netherlands, New Zealand, Portugal, Romania, Singapore, Spain, Sweden, Switzerland, United Kingdom, United States. If your store is based outside these countries, you cannot use Shopify Payments and true multi-currency charging is not available through Shopify's native system.

Failure Mode 3: Currency Not Assigned to the Market in Markets Configuration

Shopify Markets does not automatically assign local currencies when you create a market. Many merchants create a "Europe" market or "UK" market but skip the step of adding local currencies — so the market exists but customers from those regions are still shown and charged in the primary currency.

Complete Markets currency setup checklist

  1. Go to Admin → Settings → Markets
  2. Click on the market you want to configure (e.g., "Europe" or "United Kingdom")
  3. Under the "Products and pricing" section, find "Currencies"
  4. Click "Add currencies" and add the local currency (EUR, GBP, etc.) — this step is separate from creating the market
  5. Set the exchange rate: "Automatic" uses Shopify's daily rate, or set a manual rate with a fixed markup (e.g., 5% above spot rate to protect against fluctuation)
  6. Optionally set rounding rules: e.g., round to nearest .99 or .00 for price aesthetic consistency
  7. Verify the currency is now shown on the market settings page under the assigned currencies list
  8. Test by visiting the storefront in an Incognito window with a VPN set to the target country — confirm both storefront price display AND checkout currency
Market Detection — How Shopify Assigns Customers to Markets
Shopify assigns customers to markets based on: (1) IP geolocation (automatic, highest priority), (2) browser language/locale headers, (3) manual market selector if you've added one to your theme. If a customer uses a VPN with a US IP address while physically in Germany, they will be placed in the US market and shown USD prices. Shopify does not have a way to override IP-based detection on the customer's side — you can only add a manual currency/market selector as a fallback.

Failure Mode 4: Customer Charged Different Amount Than Displayed — Exchange Rate Fluctuation + Bank FX Fees

Even with Shopify Payments and Shopify Markets correctly configured, customers sometimes report being charged a different amount than what Shopify displayed. This is not a bug — it is the combined effect of two separate currency conversion steps.

The two conversion steps that cause the discrepancy

Step When It Happens Who Controls It Customer Impact
Shopify exchange rate lock When checkout session starts Shopify (daily update) Rate may differ from when customer viewed product — browsed at 1.08, checked out at 1.06
Bank FX conversion fee When bank processes the transaction Customer's bank Bank adds 1–3% above interbank rate; customer sees higher amount on bank statement than on Shopify checkout

Shopify's exchange rate behavior

Shopify updates exchange rates daily. When a customer enters checkout, Shopify locks the exchange rate for that session. This rate is held for the duration of the checkout session (10–15 minutes of inactivity before session expiry). If the customer browses for an hour, adds items to cart, then comes back the next day and completes checkout, they will get the rate from when their current checkout session started — not from when they first viewed the product.

For merchants using a manual exchange rate with a fixed markup (e.g., 5% above spot), the displayed price in local currency reflects that markup but Shopify's actual settlement rate from the payment processor may differ slightly from the spot rate on any given day.

What to communicate to customers

For display-only converter stores: include a clear note that prices shown are approximate and the final charge is in USD (or your primary currency). For Shopify Markets stores with true multi-currency: note that the amount on the bank statement may appear slightly different from the checkout total due to the customer's bank's FX processing fee.

Failure Mode 5: Currency Switch Mid-Session Resets Cart Pricing

When a customer switches currency after items are already in the cart, Shopify reprices the cart in the new currency using the current exchange rate. This creates visible price changes that confuse customers — and with display-only currency apps, it creates a worse problem.

Display-only app + cart currency mismatch

A customer adds a product to cart while viewing "€89" (displayed by the converter app). They then switch to GBP. The converter app now shows "£76." The customer proceeds to checkout and sees $97 — the original USD price. Three different numbers for the same product in one session.

With true Shopify Markets multi-currency, the cart is re-priced in the new currency at the current market rate when the customer switches. The cart total changes. This is accurate — but if the EUR price was €89.00 and the GBP price at the current rate is £76.43, the customer may assume the price changed and contact support.

Preventing mid-session currency confusion

  • If you have Shopify Markets: add a currency selector to the storefront header that sets the market context before the customer starts shopping — not after items are in the cart
  • Consider using Shopify's country_selector feature which sets both the market and currency simultaneously
  • For display-only apps: some apps offer a "lock currency to cart" option that prevents switching after cart add — check your app's settings
  • If customers are confused by mid-session repricing: a clear tooltip on the currency selector explaining "Switching currency recalculates prices at the current exchange rate" reduces support tickets
How Shopify Markets Handles Currency Switching
When a customer switches market (which triggers a currency switch in Shopify Markets), Shopify creates a new checkout context. The cart is preserved but line item prices are recalculated in the new currency using the current rate for that market. Any discounts, automatic discounts, or shipping rates that applied to the previous market context are re-evaluated against the new market's rules. This means a free shipping threshold set at "over $75 USD" may or may not trigger in the EUR market if the threshold is configured separately per market.

Failure Mode 6: Fixed-Amount Discount Codes Apply in Primary Currency, Not Local Currency Equivalent

Discount codes in Shopify are created in the store's primary currency. When used on a multi-currency store, fixed-amount discounts behave unexpectedly: a "$20 off" code gives €20 off to a European customer — not the EUR equivalent of $20.

Currency-specific discount behavior

Discount Type Multi-Currency Behavior Issue?
Percentage (e.g., 20% off) Applied to local currency price — works correctly across currencies No issue
Fixed amount (e.g., $20 off) $20 deducted from the amount in the store's primary currency — if store is USD, EUR customer gets €20 off regardless of the USD/EUR rate Causes confusion
Free shipping Applied correctly to the local currency checkout — no issue No issue
Buy X get Y (BXGY) Works correctly — product quantity logic is currency-independent No issue

Example of the fixed-amount problem

A merchant creates a "$20 off orders over $100" discount code for a US promotion. They send this code to their European email list. A European customer with a €95 order (≈ $103) applies the code. The discount deducts €20 from their order — reducing a €95 cart to €75. The merchant intended to give $20 USD off (≈ €18.40 at the current rate), but the customer received €20 off — 8.6% more value than intended. At scale this is a meaningful revenue impact.

Fix for fixed-amount discount codes on multi-currency stores

  • Prefer percentage discounts on multi-currency stores — they are inherently currency-neutral
  • For market-specific promotions, use Shopify Markets' ability to set per-market prices and create market-specific discount codes via the Shopify Admin (available on Shopify plan and above with Markets Pro on some plans)
  • For Shopify Plus, use Shopify Functions to create custom discount logic that applies per-currency fixed-amount discounts correctly
  • If you must use fixed-amount codes globally: document the expected behavior so support staff can explain the discrepancy to customers who calculate the discount differently

Failure Mode 7: Gift Card Balance Fluctuates in Local Currency — Stored in Primary Currency

Shopify gift cards are stored in the store's primary currency. When a customer redeems a gift card on a multi-currency store, Shopify converts the gift card balance to the local currency at the current exchange rate. This means the displayed gift card balance changes every day with the exchange rate — a $100 gift card may show as €92 one month and €87 the next.

Specific scenarios that confuse customers

Scenario What Customer Sees Why
Gift card issued at $100 USD when rate was 1.08 €92.59 at issuance $100 ÷ 1.08 = €92.59
Same card checked 6 months later, rate now 1.15 €86.96 remaining — no purchases made $100 ÷ 1.15 = €86.96 — "balance shrank" by €5.63
Partial use — spent $40, remaining $60 Balance shown in EUR recalculated at today's rate $60 at current rate — may differ from remaining at rate when card was issued
Gift card email from sender says "€100 gift card" Displays less than €100 at checkout if rate changed Shopify issued a $109 card (rate at issuance), now converts to €100 at new rate — accurate but confusing

What merchants can do

  • There is no way to lock a Shopify gift card's value in a non-primary currency — this is a Shopify platform limitation
  • For gift card products intended for European customers: set the gift card product's price in the European market using Shopify Markets per-market pricing — but the underlying stored value is still in primary currency
  • The most reliable fix is to issue gift cards in the customer's currency by setting up a separate Shopify store for each major currency market (complex, but eliminates the fluctuation entirely)
  • For support purposes: train your team to explain that gift card balances are stored in USD (or your primary currency) and display in local currency at the current rate

Failure Mode 8: Headless Storefront API — Missing presentmentCurrencyCode / countryCode on Cart

In headless Shopify storefronts (Hydrogen, Next.js Commerce, custom React/Vue frontends), multi-currency requires explicit configuration in every Storefront API call. Many headless implementations fetch prices and display them in local currency using JavaScript conversion — then create the Shopify cart without passing the customer's country or currency context. This produces the same display-vs-charge discrepancy as third-party converter apps.

What must be set for true headless multi-currency

The Storefront API uses buyerIdentity.countryCode on the cart to determine the presentment currency. When a cart is created with a country code, Shopify returns prices in that country's market currency. Without it, all prices return in the store's primary currency.

mutation CartCreate($input: CartInput!) {
  cartCreate(input: $input) {
    cart {
      id
      cost {
        totalAmount {
          amount
          currencyCode   # Will be EUR if buyerIdentity.countryCode = "DE"
        }
      }
    }
  }
}

# Input with country code:
{
  "input": {
    "buyerIdentity": {
      "countryCode": "DE"   # Required for EUR presentment
    },
    "lines": [
      {
        "merchandiseId": "gid://shopify/ProductVariant/...",
        "quantity": 1
      }
    ]
  }
}

Common headless multi-currency mistakes

Mistake Symptom Fix
Prices fetched via products query without @inContext(country: DE) Product pages show USD prices — currency converter app overrides with JS Add @inContext(country: $countryCode) directive to all product price queries
Cart created without buyerIdentity.countryCode Storefront shows EUR, cart shows USD, checkout charges USD Pass buyerIdentity: { countryCode: "DE" } in cartCreate mutation
cartBuyerIdentityUpdate not called when customer changes country/currency Cart currency stuck at initial country, price doesn't update on currency switch Call cartBuyerIdentityUpdate mutation with new countryCode when customer changes market
Reading priceV2 instead of presentmentPrices on cart lines Cart totals in USD even when country code set correctly Use cost.totalAmount and cost.subtotalAmount on the cart object — these reflect the presentment currency
Checkout URL opened without preserving cart context Cart currency set to EUR, checkout opens and reverts to USD Ensure the cart's checkoutUrl is used directly — do not reconstruct a checkout URL manually

Verifying headless multi-currency is working end-to-end

  1. After creating a cart with buyerIdentity.countryCode: "DE", query the cart and check cost.totalAmount.currencyCode — it should return "EUR", not "USD"
  2. Use the cart's checkoutUrl field to redirect to checkout — verify the checkout page shows prices in EUR
  3. Complete a test order with a test card — verify the order in Admin shows the EUR amount in the order details
  4. Check Admin → Orders → the specific order: if the order currency field shows EUR and the order total is in EUR, true multi-currency is working; if it shows USD, the cart country code was not set
Hydrogen (Shopify's Official Headless Framework) Multi-Currency
Shopify's Hydrogen framework handles multi-currency via the I18nLocale type and the countries config in app/lib/context.ts. The storefront.query utility automatically appends @inContext directives when a country is set in the locale context. If you are building on Hydrogen and multi-currency is not working, check that the locale is being set from the URL path or subdomain, and that cartCreate is passing buyerIdentity from the session. The Hydrogen demo store source code on GitHub is a reliable reference implementation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use Stripe for payments and still charge customers in local currencies?

No. Shopify's native multi-currency charging (where the customer is actually billed in EUR, GBP, etc.) only works with Shopify Payments. If you use Stripe as your payment gateway through Shopify, all charges settle in your store's primary currency — the EUR price shown on the storefront is a display conversion only. If you want to use Stripe and support true local currency charging, you would need to build a custom headless checkout directly on Stripe's API (outside of Shopify's checkout), which is a significant engineering undertaking and loses Shopify's fraud protection, order management, and native checkout features.

Why does my Markets currency work for some products but not others?

If some products show and charge correctly in EUR while others revert to USD, the most likely cause is that those specific products have product-level pricing overrides set for the market. In Shopify Markets, you can set per-market prices for individual products (Admin → Products → [Product] → Pricing → Markets-specific pricing). If a product-level override is set in USD instead of EUR, that product will use the USD override rather than the market's automatic currency conversion. Check each affected product for market-specific pricing overrides and either remove them (to use automatic conversion) or update them to the correct local currency amounts.

Why does the currency shown in checkout not match the currency in the confirmation email?

This usually happens when the order is captured in one currency but the email template is not configured for multi-currency display. Shopify's default order confirmation email uses {{ order.total_price | money }} which should display in the order's currency. If your email template uses a custom money filter or hardcodes a currency symbol (e.g., $), it will show USD even on EUR orders. Check your notification email templates in Admin → Settings → Notifications → Order confirmation and ensure the Liquid money filters are not overriding the currency symbol.

Can I show one currency in all countries but charge in local currency?

No. With Shopify Markets and Shopify Payments, the currency displayed to the customer is the same currency they are charged in — Shopify does not support showing USD prices but charging in EUR. If you want all customers to see prices in USD regardless of location (for simplicity or brand consistency), you should use your primary currency as USD and not add local currencies to your markets. Customers from EUR countries will see USD prices and their bank will convert at the bank's FX rate when processing the charge.
