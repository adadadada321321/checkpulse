Shopify Meeza Not Working — Card Declined, 19-Digit Number Rejected & 3DS OTP Issues

Updated June 2026 · 7 failure modes · Egypt merchants · Meeza national card scheme · CBE · EGP only

Meeza (ميزة) is Egypt's national domestic payment card scheme, established by the Central Bank of Egypt and issued by all major Egyptian banks. When Meeza stops working on Shopify, the failure mode is everything — a 19-digit card number rejected by the checkout form is a completely different problem from a gateway that doesn't support Egyptian domestic cards, which is different from a customer whose card is not activated for internet purchases, which is different from a 3DS OTP that won't arrive. Understanding which of the seven failure modes you are facing is the difference between a 5-minute configuration change and days of incorrect debugging with the wrong team.

How Meeza Works — and Why It Fails Differently From Visa/Mastercard

Meeza is Egypt's national domestic card scheme, not a variant of Visa or Mastercard. The Central Bank of Egypt launched Meeza in 2019 to create a sovereign national payment network for Egypt, reducing dependence on international card schemes for domestic transactions. Meeza cards are issued by Egyptian banks — Banque Misr, National Bank of Egypt, Bank of Alexandria, CIB, QNB Egypt, Banque du Caire, and others — and can be physical debit or prepaid cards, or digital prepaid cards (Meeza Digital) accessible via mobile banking apps.

Meeza cards use 19-digit card numbers, not the standard 16 digits used by most Visa and Mastercard cards. This single difference is one of the most common and easily missed causes of Meeza checkout failures on Shopify. Standard checkout card number fields — including default Shopify templates and many payment gateway forms — enforce a 16-digit maximum. A customer entering their 19-digit Meeza card number will see a validation error before the payment is even attempted, but the error message ("invalid card number") gives no indication that the card length, not the card itself, is the problem.

Meeza transactions are processed on Egypt's national payment network (EBC — Egyptian Banks Company), not on Visa or Mastercard's global networks. Payment gateways must have explicit connectivity to Egypt's domestic network to process Meeza cards. International gateways like Stripe and PayPal that serve primarily non-Egyptian merchants are not connected to the domestic Egyptian network and will decline Meeza cards. Egypt-focused gateways — Paymob, Kashier, PayTabs Egypt, and Fawry Pay — support Meeza natively.

Some Meeza cards are co-badged with Visa or Mastercard. A co-badged Meeza card displays both the Meeza logo and a Visa or Mastercard logo and can be processed on either the domestic Meeza network or the international Visa/Mastercard network. Co-badged cards may work with international gateways via the Visa/Mastercard route — but purely domestic Meeza cards without a co-badge will only work with Egypt-local gateways.

1

Payment gateway does not support Meeza / Egyptian domestic cards

Merchant Issue

The most common reason Meeza cards fail across all customers at a Shopify store is that the merchant's payment gateway is not connected to Egypt's domestic card network. Meeza transactions are processed through the Egyptian Banks Company (EBC) domestic interbank network, which requires a payment gateway to have a direct acquiring relationship with an Egyptian bank or direct connectivity to the EBC network. Most international payment gateways do not have this connection.

When a customer enters a Meeza card at a store using an incompatible gateway, one of three things happens: the transaction is declined outright with a generic "card declined" or "payment could not be processed" error; the card number fails validation because the gateway does not recognize Egyptian domestic BIN ranges; or — for co-badged Meeza/Visa cards — the gateway attempts to route the transaction via the Visa network, which may succeed or fail depending on the specific card and issuing bank's configuration for international card-present routing.

Payment gateways that natively support Meeza for Egyptian merchants include Paymob (the most widely used), Kashier (Egypt-founded fintech with strong Meeza support), PayTabs Egypt (regional gateway with Egypt domestic support), and Fawry Pay (primarily known for offline, but also supports online Meeza processing). These gateways have local Egyptian bank acquiring relationships and are connected to the EBC network. Using one of these gateways is the most reliable way to accept Meeza cards from Egyptian customers on Shopify.

Merchants who already have a gateway that works for international card payments (Visa/Mastercard) often assume Meeza support comes automatically. It does not — Meeza is a separate card scheme requiring a separate acquiring connection. Some gateways offer Meeza as an add-on to an existing account; confirm with your gateway provider whether Meeza domestic processing is included in your current plan or requires an upgrade or separate application.

How to fix

  1. Confirm whether your current gateway supports Meeza: Contact your payment gateway support and ask explicitly whether your current plan includes Meeza domestic card processing (not just Visa/Mastercard). Ask for the specific Meeza BIN ranges they process and whether they have an acquiring agreement with an Egyptian bank for domestic card transactions.
  2. Switch to an Egypt-local gateway if needed: If your current gateway does not support Meeza, integrate one of the Egypt-focused gateways alongside your existing international gateway. Paymob supports both international cards (via Visa/Mastercard) and domestic Meeza cards through a single integration, making it a practical choice for Egyptian merchants who need both. Kashier is another Egypt-founded alternative with strong Meeza support and a Shopify plugin.
  3. Install the gateway's Shopify plugin: Both Paymob and Kashier have Shopify payment app integrations available in the Shopify App Store or via custom payment provider registration. Follow the gateway's Shopify integration documentation carefully — gateway configuration requires entering API keys, integration IDs, and enabling specific payment methods including Meeza within the gateway dashboard.
  4. Enable Meeza specifically within the gateway dashboard: After integrating a compatible gateway, log in to the gateway's merchant dashboard and confirm that Meeza cards (sometimes listed as "domestic cards" or by the specific Meeza product name) are enabled as an accepted payment method. Enabling the gateway for Visa/Mastercard does not automatically enable Meeza.
  5. Test with a real Meeza card: Complete a low-value test transaction using a real Meeza card (physical or Meeza Digital) through an incognito browser to confirm end-to-end processing. Do not rely on sandbox test cards — test with an actual Meeza-issued card from an Egyptian bank.
2

19-digit Meeza card number rejected by checkout form validation

Merchant Issue

Meeza cards issued by Egyptian banks have 19-digit card numbers. The ISO 7812 standard permits card numbers from 12 to 19 digits, but the vast majority of payment cards in global circulation use 16 digits. As a result, many checkout implementations — including Shopify's default card form and numerous third-party payment gateway widgets — enforce a maximum of 16 digits on the card number input. A customer entering their 19-digit Meeza number will see the card number field reject the input or display a validation error before any payment attempt is made.

This is a purely technical, merchant-side issue: the customer's card and bank account are completely valid, and the card would be approved if the number could be submitted. The error message typically displayed — "invalid card number," "card number is too long," or "please check your card number" — gives the customer no indication that the problem is the field length limit rather than a problem with their card. Customers commonly call their bank assuming the card is blocked, or abandon the purchase entirely, when the actual fix is a configuration change on the merchant's checkout form.

The 19-digit issue affects not only the card number field length but also the Luhn check implementation. Some checkout form validators apply the Luhn algorithm only to 16-digit numbers — a valid 19-digit Meeza card will fail these validators even if the field length is increased, because the Luhn check implementation itself doesn't handle 19-digit numbers correctly. The correct implementation is to apply the Luhn algorithm to card numbers of any valid ISO 7812 length (12–19 digits).

For merchants using Paymob, Kashier, or PayTabs Egypt as their gateway, the hosted payment page or embedded payment widget provided by these gateways should already handle 19-digit Meeza numbers correctly — these gateways were built with Egyptian domestic cards in mind. The problem is most common when merchants use a custom checkout card input, a generic Shopify card form, or a gateway widget designed primarily for international markets.

How to fix

  1. Identify where the card number validation is enforced: Open your Shopify checkout in a browser, open developer tools, and inspect the card number input field. Look for a `maxlength` attribute on the input (should be at least 19 for Meeza support) and any JavaScript validation code that enforces a 16-digit limit. The validation may be in the gateway widget's JavaScript, a Shopify checkout customization, or a theme script.
  2. Use the gateway's hosted payment page: The simplest fix is to use a payment gateway that provides its own hosted payment page or iframe widget (Paymob's hosted checkout, Kashier's payment widget) rather than a custom card form. These gateways handle Meeza 19-digit card numbers natively and keep validation complexity out of your Shopify theme. Redirect-based checkout models (where the customer is sent to the gateway's page to enter card details) automatically resolve the card number length issue.
  3. Increase card number field maxlength if using a custom form: If you maintain a custom card input field, update the `maxlength` attribute to 19. Update any JavaScript validation that checks `cardNumber.length === 16` to instead check `cardNumber.length >= 12 && cardNumber.length <= 19`.
  4. Update the Luhn check to handle variable-length numbers: Ensure your Luhn algorithm implementation runs correctly on any card number between 12 and 19 digits, not only on 16-digit numbers. Test with a known valid 19-digit Meeza test number provided by your gateway.
  5. Format the card number display correctly: Standard 16-digit card number display formats use groups of 4 (1234 5678 9012 3456). Meeza's 19-digit number groups as 4-4-4-4-3 (1234 5678 9012 3456 789). Update any card number auto-formatting in your checkout to use the correct grouping for detected Meeza BIN ranges.
Merchant note: The 19-digit Meeza card issue is invisible in your payment logs — the card number is rejected on the client side before a payment request is ever sent to your gateway. If Egyptian customers are abandoning your checkout without a payment error in your gateway logs, the 19-digit validation failure is a likely cause. Add client-side logging or a session recording tool to diagnose front-end checkout abandonment.
3

3DS OTP not arriving or bank authentication failing

Merchant + Customer

Meeza card online payments require 3D Secure (3DS) authentication — the issuing bank sends a one-time password (OTP) to the cardholder's registered mobile number or banking application, and the customer must enter it within the authentication window to complete the transaction. If the OTP does not arrive or the 3DS step fails, the payment is declined with an authentication failure, typically displayed to the customer as "payment authentication failed" or "your bank declined this transaction."

The most common 3DS OTP failure causes are on the customer side: the mobile number registered at the issuing bank is outdated (a number the customer no longer has active), the SIM associated with the registered number is in a different phone or deactivated, the customer is traveling or has international roaming disabled, or the bank's SMS delivery is delayed and the OTP expires before it arrives. For Meeza Digital cardholders (digital prepaid cards), the OTP may be delivered through the bank's mobile app notification rather than by SMS — customers who do not have the app installed or notification permissions enabled will not receive it.

On the merchant side, 3DS failures can also originate from incorrect gateway configuration. If the payment gateway is not properly configured to redirect customers to the bank's 3DS authentication page — or if the gateway's 3DS callback URL is misconfigured, causing the bank's authentication result to not reach the gateway — transactions will fail at the 3DS step even when the OTP is received successfully. This manifests as all Meeza card payments failing at the authentication step regardless of which customer's card is used.

A separate category of 3DS failure occurs when the issuing bank's 3DS authentication server has downtime or a technical issue. During such periods, all online Meeza card transactions from that bank will fail 3DS regardless of correct OTP entry. These failures are typically brief and resolve on their own — but if multiple customers from the same bank experience simultaneous 3DS failures, the bank's authentication server is the likely culprit rather than any merchant-side issue.

How to fix

  1. Distinguish between customer-side and merchant-side 3DS failures: Review your gateway's payment logs for the specific 3DS failure code. An authentication failure code indicating "customer did not authenticate" or "OTP not entered" is a customer-side issue. A failure code indicating "authentication server unreachable" or "3DS callback not received" is a merchant-side gateway configuration issue.
  2. Verify gateway 3DS callback URL configuration: Log in to your payment gateway dashboard and confirm that the 3DS callback URL (the URL where the bank sends authentication results back to your gateway) is correctly configured and publicly reachable. An incorrect or unreachable callback URL causes all 3DS transactions to fail even when the OTP is entered correctly.
  3. Test 3DS flow end-to-end: Complete a Meeza card test transaction with 3DS enabled, using a card from a specific Egyptian bank. Confirm that the authentication popup or redirect appears, that the OTP arrives (via SMS or bank app), and that entering it successfully completes the payment. If the popup does not appear, the redirect configuration needs investigation.
  4. Advise customers with OTP issues: If individual customers report not receiving their OTP, advise them to: (a) confirm their bank has their current mobile number on file — update via bank app or branch if outdated; (b) check whether their phone can receive international SMS if they are abroad; (c) for Meeza Digital, open the bank app and check for an in-app notification or OTP in the notifications section; (d) contact their issuing bank to confirm 3DS enrollment and mobile number registration.
  5. Provide alternative payment options during bank outages: When the source of failure is a specific bank's 3DS server being temporarily down, there is no fix on the merchant side. Offer customers alternative payment methods (Fawry offline code, other card types) during the outage. The bank's 3DS server typically recovers within a few hours.
4

Meeza card not activated for internet (online) purchases

Customer Issue

Egyptian banks issue Meeza debit and prepaid cards with online (internet) purchase functionality disabled by default. This is a security measure — the card can be used at ATMs and physical POS terminals without any additional activation, but e-commerce purchases require the cardholder to explicitly enable internet transactions. Until the customer enables online purchases, every attempt to use the card on Shopify or any other online store will be declined by the issuing bank with a "card not permitted for this transaction type" or equivalent rejection, regardless of whether the merchant's gateway and checkout are correctly configured for Meeza.

This default-disabled behavior is common across Egyptian banks for both Meeza debit cards and Meeza prepaid cards. The customer is typically notified at card issuance that online purchases must be enabled separately, but this is often communicated in fine print or overlooked — particularly by customers receiving their first bank card. When they try to use the new card online and it is declined, their first assumption is usually that the merchant's website or payment system has a problem.

The activation process varies by bank but is typically straightforward: most major Egyptian banks allow customers to enable internet purchases via their mobile banking app, internet banking portal, or by calling the bank's customer service number. Some banks also allow activation via ATM. The change takes effect immediately or within minutes of activation. Banks including Banque Misr, National Bank of Egypt, CIB, and Bank of Alexandria all provide in-app internet purchase toggle functionality in their mobile banking applications.

From the merchant's perspective, a card-not-activated decline looks identical to any other bank-side decline. The decline code in the gateway response may say "do not honor," "card restricted," or "transaction not permitted to cardholder" — these codes do not specifically indicate the internet purchase restriction. The only way to distinguish this from other decline causes is to confirm with the customer that they have used the card for online purchases before (if not, activation is likely needed) and to advise them to check their bank app settings.

How to fix

  1. Add internet purchase guidance to your checkout: If Meeza is a significant payment method for your customers, add a visible note near the Meeza card option at checkout: "First time using Meeza online? Enable internet purchases in your bank app before checking out." This prevents customer confusion without requiring any change to your payment processing setup.
  2. Guide declined customers to activate their card: When a customer contacts you about a Meeza card decline that does not have an obvious other cause, guide them through the card activation process. The instructions differ by bank, but for the major banks: Banque Misr — open Banque Misr app → Cards → select card → Internet Purchases → Enable; National Bank of Egypt — NBE Mobile → Cards → Settings → Online Purchase Activation; CIB — CIB Mobile → Cards → Manage Card → Enable E-Commerce; Bank of Alexandria — contact customer service or bank branch.
  3. Include bank-specific activation links in your order failure email: If your store sends order failure emails when a payment is declined, consider including a brief note for Egyptian customers explaining how to enable internet purchases on a Meeza card, with links to the major banks' app download pages. This can recover abandoned orders from customers who were simply unaware of the activation requirement.
  4. Offer alternative payment methods: Until customers activate their Meeza card for online purchases, offer Fawry offline payment, Meeza Digital (digital prepaid card issued via bank app — may have different default settings), or cash on delivery as fallbacks for customers who cannot immediately enable internet purchases.
Merchant note: Internet purchase activation issues are especially common immediately after Egyptian bank promotions or campaigns where customers receive new Meeza cards (salary card migrations, student card programs, bank account upgrades). If you notice a spike in Meeza card declines without other changes to your configuration, check whether a major bank has recently issued a large batch of new cards to a population that includes your customer base.
5

Using Stripe or PayPal — international gateways that don't accept Meeza

Merchant Issue

Stripe and PayPal are the two most commonly integrated payment gateways on Shopify globally, and many Egyptian merchants begin with one of them due to their reputation, documentation quality, and easy setup. However, neither Stripe nor PayPal natively processes Egyptian domestic Meeza card transactions. These gateways are connected to Visa, Mastercard, American Express, and select local payment methods in specific countries — but as of 2026, neither has a domestic Egyptian card acquiring agreement that would allow Meeza-only card processing.

Stripe's country support for Egypt means Stripe can be used for processing transactions on internationally-networked cards (Visa and Mastercard issued by Egyptian banks for international use), but not for Meeza domestic cards. A customer in Egypt with a Visa-badged card issued by an Egyptian bank may succeed with Stripe; a customer whose Meeza card has no international co-badge will fail. The inconsistency — some Egyptian cards working and others not — is often the first sign that the gateway supports international networks but not the domestic Meeza scheme.

The same distinction applies to PayPal: a customer who links their Meeza card to a PayPal account may find the card rejected during PayPal's card verification step, or the card may link successfully but fail at the payment step if PayPal cannot process the domestic transaction. PayPal's Meeza support situation in Egypt is similarly limited to co-badged cards that can be routed via Mastercard's network.

The correct approach for Egyptian merchants who want to serve both international customers (with international Visa/Mastercard) and local Egyptian customers (with domestic Meeza cards) is to use an Egypt-local gateway that supports both. Paymob is the most widely used solution — it processes international Visa/Mastercard and domestic Meeza cards through a single integration, replacing the need for both an international gateway and a separate domestic solution.

How to fix

  1. If you serve primarily Egyptian customers: Replace or supplement Stripe/PayPal with Paymob, Kashier, or PayTabs Egypt. These gateways natively support Meeza domestic cards. Paymob also processes Visa and Mastercard, making it a single-gateway solution for both local and international cards.
  2. If you serve both Egyptian and international customers: Use a two-gateway setup — keep Stripe or PayPal for international customers with internationally-networked cards, and add Paymob or Kashier as a second payment option specifically for Egyptian domestic card holders. Display both options at checkout: Stripe/PayPal for international customers, Paymob for Egyptian customers paying with Meeza.
  3. Check for co-badge support before dismissing Stripe entirely: If a significant portion of your Egyptian customers already have co-badged Meeza/Visa or Meeza/Mastercard cards, Stripe may process some of those transactions successfully via the international card network. Run a sample of declined Meeza cards and check whether any would succeed with Stripe if routed via the co-badge. This helps calibrate how much incremental revenue a local gateway adds for your specific customer mix.
  4. Integrate Paymob's Shopify payment provider: Paymob offers a Shopify payment app integration. Follow Paymob's Shopify integration guide to add it as a payment provider in your Shopify admin → Settings → Payments → Third-party providers. Configure your Paymob integration ID and API credentials, enable the Meeza card acceptance option in your Paymob dashboard, and test with a real Meeza card.
Critical: Do not migrate away from Stripe/PayPal for international customers. A gateway switch that accidentally removes a working international card option while adding Meeza support will lose more revenue than it gains. Test the new gateway addition in parallel with your existing gateway before removing anything.
6

Meeza Digital not working or not appearing at checkout

Merchant + Customer

Meeza Digital is a digital prepaid card issued by Egyptian banks through their mobile banking applications. It carries a 19-digit Meeza card number, has its own card expiry date and CVV, and can be used for online purchases the same way a physical card is used — by entering the card details at checkout. Meeza Digital is not a separate payment method that appears in your payment method list; it is used through the standard card payment flow by entering the digital card's number, expiry, and CVV.

If Meeza Digital is not working at checkout, the failure modes are largely the same as for physical Meeza cards: the 19-digit card number is rejected by the checkout form; the gateway does not support Egyptian domestic cards; the 3DS OTP is delivered through the bank app rather than SMS (meaning the customer must have the app open and notifications enabled); or the card's prepaid balance is insufficient for the order total. The prepaid balance issue is unique to Meeza Digital — unlike a debit card linked to a bank account, Meeza Digital requires the customer to have loaded funds in advance. A checkout decline that looks like a payment failure may simply be an insufficient balance that the customer hasn't noticed.

Some customers believe that Meeza Digital requires a separate integration or that it "should appear as a wallet option" at checkout like Aman Wallet or Fawry. It does not — Meeza Digital is used as a standard card payment by entering the card number. If the customer is expecting a separate button or option for Meeza Digital, they should instead use the standard card number entry field and enter their Meeza Digital card details there.

One issue specific to Meeza Digital is card expiry: Meeza Digital cards issued by some banks have short validity periods (one year or less) compared to physical cards. A customer who received a Meeza Digital card months ago and has not checked the expiry date may try to use an expired card, which will be declined. Unlike a physical card in a wallet, digital cards can expire unnoticed if the customer has not revisited the bank app recently.

How to fix

  1. Clarify Meeza Digital usage in your checkout: Add a brief tooltip or help text near your card payment option explaining that Meeza Digital cards are entered as standard card numbers — there is no separate Meeza Digital button. Provide an example showing the 19-digit card number format to orient customers.
  2. Guide customers to check their prepaid balance: If a Meeza Digital customer reports a decline with no obvious technical cause, advise them to open their bank app and check the Meeza Digital card balance before retrying. A low balance is the most common non-technical cause of Meeza Digital declines.
  3. Handle the 19-digit card number at the checkout form level: Ensure your checkout form accepts 19-digit card numbers (see Failure Mode 2). Meeza Digital has the same 19-digit format as physical Meeza cards and will fail the same front-end validation if the form enforces a 16-digit limit.
  4. Advise customers on Meeza Digital 3DS: Meeza Digital 3DS authentication may deliver the OTP in-app (as a push notification or in-app message) rather than by SMS, depending on the issuing bank. Advise customers to have their banking app open and notifications enabled when completing checkout, so they receive the 3DS OTP immediately.
  5. Check card expiry: Advise customers to verify their Meeza Digital card's expiry date in the bank app. If expired, the customer needs to request a new digital card from the bank before using it online.

Currency not EGP — foreign currency transaction on EGP-only card

Merchant Issue

Meeza cards are Egyptian Pound (EGP) cards. Physical domestic Meeza cards and Meeza Digital cards issued by Egyptian banks are denominated in EGP and primarily intended for use with merchants priced in EGP. When a Shopify store prices products in USD, EUR, GBP, or another foreign currency, and a customer attempts to pay with a domestic Meeza card, one of several failure modes occurs depending on the gateway and the card's settings.

For domestic Meeza cards without an international co-badge (Visa or Mastercard), the card simply cannot process foreign currency transactions at all. These cards are restricted to EGP transactions on the domestic Egyptian network, and a foreign currency purchase will be declined regardless of the gateway or the store's configuration. The decline will typically appear as "transaction currency not supported" or "card not permitted for foreign currency transactions."

For co-badged Meeza/Visa or Meeza/Mastercard cards that have some international transaction capability, a foreign currency purchase may be attempted but will involve a currency conversion — the amount is converted from the purchase currency to EGP at the current exchange rate and charged to the cardholder in EGP. This conversion may happen at the bank level with an additional foreign transaction fee that the customer did not expect, causing confusion about the final amount charged.

The safest configuration for a Shopify store targeting Egyptian customers is to price in EGP. If the store serves both Egyptian and international customers, use Shopify Markets to display EGP pricing for Egyptian customers specifically. Ensuring the checkout currency is EGP before Meeza card payment processing prevents all currency-related Meeza failures and eliminates unexpected foreign transaction fees for customers.

How to fix

  1. Price in EGP for Egyptian customers: If your store primarily serves Egyptian customers, set the store's base currency to EGP in Shopify admin → Settings → Store details → Store currency. If you serve both Egyptian and international customers, use Shopify Markets to configure EGP as the presented currency for customers visiting from Egypt, while maintaining other currencies for international visitors.
  2. Confirm gateway currency configuration: Log in to your payment gateway dashboard and confirm that EGP is configured as an accepted processing currency. Some gateways require explicit activation of EGP as a settlement currency — a gateway configured only for USD or EUR processing will convert EGP payments, potentially causing discrepancies or failures.
  3. Restrict Meeza to EGP checkout only: If your store supports multiple currencies, configure Meeza card acceptance to appear only when the checkout currency is EGP. When a customer has selected a non-EGP currency, hide the Meeza payment option and show only internationally-compatible payment methods. This prevents failed foreign currency Meeza attempts and confusion.
  4. Communicate EGP pricing clearly: Display prices prominently in EGP on product pages and in the cart. If your store auto-converts prices to the customer's local currency, ensure Egyptian customers see EGP amounts, not USD or EUR equivalents that may trigger currency mismatch issues at the Meeza card level.
Critical: Domestic Meeza cards (without Visa/Mastercard co-badge) cannot process foreign currency transactions at all. Do not advise customers with domestic-only Meeza cards to try a different browser, try later, or contact their bank to "enable foreign purchases" — the fundamental restriction is the card type. Advise these customers to use a co-badged card or an alternative payment method like Fawry or bank transfer if their Meeza card is domestic-only and the store is not priced in EGP.
